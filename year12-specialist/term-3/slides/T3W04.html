Key idea

A new sample gives a new \(\bar{x}\) — so \(\bar{X}\) has a distribution of its own. Its centre matches the population, but its spread shrinks like \(1/\sqrt{n}\).

Lesson 1 · 15D

The sample mean is a random variable

  • Derive \(E(\bar{X}) = \mu\) and \(\text{sd}(\bar{X}) = \sigma/\sqrt{n}\) from last week's rules.
  • Normal population → \(\bar{X}\) exactly normal, for every \(n\).
  • Compare individual vs average probabilities.

\(\bar{X}\) as a linear combination

\[\bar{X} = \frac{X_1 + X_2 + \cdots + X_n}{n} \qquad\Rightarrow\qquad E(\bar{X}) = \mu, \qquad \text{Var}(\bar{X}) = \frac{\sigma^2}{n}, \qquad \text{sd}(\bar{X}) = \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}\]

  • This is last week's algebra: \(\text{Var}\left(\dfrac{1}{n}\sum X_i\right) = \dfrac{1}{n^2} \cdot n\sigma^2 = \dfrac{\sigma^2}{n}\).
  • If the population is normal, \(N(\mu, \sigma^2)\), then \(\bar{X}\) is exactly normal: \(\bar{X} \sim N\!\left(\mu, \dfrac{\sigma^2}{n}\right)\).
  • Quadrupling the sample size halves the standard deviation of \(\bar{X}\) — precision is bought at a square-root rate.

Worked example: individual vs average

Example 1

IQ scores are \(N(100,\, 15^2)\). Nine people are chosen at random. Find \(P(\bar{X} > 105)\), and compare with \(P(X > 105)\) for one person.

  • \(\bar{X} \sim N\!\left(100,\, \dfrac{15^2}{9}\right) = N(100,\, 5^2)\) — exactly normal, since the population is.
  • \(P(\bar{X} > 105) = P\!\left(Z > \dfrac{105-100}{5}\right) = P(Z > 1) \approx 0.159\).
  • One person: \(P(X > 105) = P\!\left(Z > \dfrac{1}{3}\right) \approx 0.369\).
Interpretation

An average of nine is much less likely to stray 5 points from the mean than a single individual is. Averages smooth out extremes.

The distribution of \(\bar{X}\) narrows as \(n\) grows

The area beyond \(\bar{x} = 105\) shrinks from 0.369 (\(n=1\)) to 0.252 (\(n=4\)) to 0.159 (\(n=9\))

Checkpoint 1 — your turn

Try it! (≈5 min)

Battery lifetimes are \(N(50,\, 6^2)\) hours. A random sample of 36 batteries is tested.

  • (a) State the distribution of \(\bar{X}\).
  • (b) Find \(P(\bar{X} < 48)\), and compare with \(P(X < 48)\) for a single battery.
  • (a) \(\bar{X} \sim N\!\left(50,\, \dfrac{36}{36}\right) = N(50,\, 1^2)\).
  • (b) \(P(\bar{X} < 48) = P(Z < -2) \approx 0.023\); one battery: \(P(X < 48) = P(Z < -\tfrac13) \approx 0.369\).
Lesson 2 · 15D

Totals and averages

  • Totals are sample means in disguise: \(T = n\bar{X}\).
  • Solve capacity problems via \(T\) or via \(\bar{X}\) — same \(z\), same answer.
  • Dodge the \(n\sigma^2\) vs \((n\sigma)^2\) trap.

Worked example: totals are sample means in disguise

Example 2

A lift's capacity is 1200 kg. Sixteen adults board; adult masses are \(N(70,\, 10^2)\) kg, independent. Find the probability the lift is overloaded.

  • Total \(T = X_1 + \cdots + X_{16}\): \(E(T) = 16 \times 70 = 1120\), \(\text{Var}(T) = 16 \times 100 = 1600\), so \(T \sim N(1120,\, 40^2)\).
  • \(P(T > 1200) = P\!\left(Z > \dfrac{1200 - 1120}{40}\right) = P(Z > 2) \approx 0.023\).
  • Equivalent route: \(T > 1200 \iff \bar{X} > 75\), and \(\bar{X} \sim N(70,\, 2.5^2)\) — same \(z\), same answer.
Note

Var of the total is \(n\sigma^2 = 1600\), not \((n\sigma)^2 = 25600\). Sixteen independent people, not one person weighed sixteen times.

Checkpoint 2 — your turn

Try it! (≈5 min)

Egg masses are \(N(60,\, 4^2)\) g, independent. A carton holds 12 eggs.

  • (a) State the distribution of the carton's total mass \(T\) (ignore the carton itself).
  • (b) Find \(P(T > 740)\).
  • (c) A shop rejects cartons whose average egg mass is below 58 g. What proportion is rejected?
  • (a) \(E(T) = 720\), \(\text{Var}(T) = 12 \times 16 = 192\): \(T \sim N(720,\, 192)\), sd \(\approx 13.86\) g.
  • (b) \(P(T > 740) = P\!\left(Z > \dfrac{20}{13.86}\right) = P(Z > 1.443) \approx 0.074\).
  • (c) \(\bar{X} \sim N\!\left(60,\, \tfrac{16}{12}\right)\), sd \(\approx 1.155\); \(P(\bar{X} < 58) = P(Z < -1.732) \approx 0.042\).
Lesson 3 · 15E–15F

Simulation and the central limit theorem

  • Watch the distribution of \(\bar{X}\) take shape by simulation.
  • State the CLT precisely — what it claims, and what it doesn't.
  • Know when the normal claim is exact vs approximate.

Simulating \(\bar{X}\)

  • Take a decidedly non-normal population — say rolls of a die, or a skewed waiting-time distribution.
  • Draw a sample of size \(n\), record \(\bar{x}\). Repeat a few thousand times and histogram the results.
  • What you see: the histogram of sample means is centred at \(\mu\), has spread \(\approx \sigma/\sqrt{n}\), and looks more normal as \(n\) grows — regardless of the population's shape.
Try it! (CAS / spreadsheet)

Simulate 1000 samples of size \(n = 2, 5, 30\) from rolls of a fair die (\(\mu = 3.5\), \(\sigma^2 = \tfrac{35}{12}\)). Histogram the means. At what \(n\) does the bell shape convince you?

The central limit theorem

Theorem (informal)

For a random sample of size \(n\) from any distribution with mean \(\mu\) and variance \(\sigma^2\), the distribution of \(\bar{X}\) is approximately \(N\!\left(\mu,\, \dfrac{\sigma^2}{n}\right)\) when \(n\) is large.

  • Working guideline: \(n \geq 30\) is usually "large enough" (sooner if the population is roughly symmetric).
  • Normal population → \(\bar{X}\) exactly normal for every \(n\); CLT is only needed when the population isn't normal.
  • This is what makes next week possible: inference about \(\mu\) without knowing the population's shape.

Seeing it: a skewed population, a normal-looking mean

Waiting times (skewed, mean 1, sd 1) — yet \(\bar{X}\) for \(n = 30\) is already close to \(N(1,\, 1/30)\). The population stays skewed; only \(\bar{X}\) turns normal.

Checkpoint 3 — true or false?

Try it! (≈4 min)
  • (a) \(\bar{X}\) is exactly normal only when the population is normal.
  • (b) The CLT says the population becomes normal when \(n\) is large.
  • (c) \(\text{sd}(\bar{X}) = \sigma/n\).
  • (d) For a normal population and \(n = 4\), we may use \(\bar{X} \sim N(\mu, \sigma^2/4)\) without invoking the CLT.
  • (a) True.   (b) False — only the distribution of \(\bar{X}\) is approximately normal; the population never changes.
  • (c) False — \(\sigma/\sqrt{n}\).   (d) True: exactness comes from the normal population, no CLT needed.
Lesson 4 · 15F

Normal approximation to the binomial

  • Why the CLT applies to binomial counts.
  • \(X \mathrel{\dot\sim} N(np,\, np(1-p))\) for large \(n\) — and the guideline for "large".
  • The sample proportion \(\hat{P}\) version.

Normal approximation to the binomial

  • \(X \sim \text{Bi}(n, p)\) is a sum of \(n\) independent 0/1 variables — so the CLT applies to it.
  • For large \(n\): \(X \mathrel{\dot\sim} N\big(np,\; np(1-p)\big)\). Guideline: fine when \(np\) and \(n(1-p)\) are both \(> 5\) or so.
Example 3

A fair coin is tossed 100 times. Estimate \(P(X \geq 60)\).

  • \(np = 50\), \(np(1-p) = 25\): \(X \mathrel{\dot\sim} N(50,\, 5^2)\).
  • \(P(X \geq 60) \approx P\!\left(Z \geq \dfrac{60-50}{5}\right) = P(Z \geq 2) \approx 0.023\). (Exact binomial: 0.028 — close, and far quicker.)
Sample proportion

Dividing by \(n\): \(\hat{P} = X/n \mathrel{\dot\sim} N\big(p,\, \tfrac{p(1-p)}{n}\big)\) — the same fact in proportion form.

Checkpoint 4 — your turn

Try it! (≈5 min)

A fair die is rolled 180 times; \(X\) counts the sixes.

  • (a) State the exact distribution of \(X\) and its mean and variance.
  • (b) Use a normal approximation to estimate \(P(X \geq 40)\).
  • (c) Why is the approximation justified here?
  • (a) \(X \sim \text{Bi}(180,\, \tfrac16)\): mean \(np = 30\), variance \(np(1-p) = 25\).
  • (b) \(X \mathrel{\dot\sim} N(30,\, 5^2)\): \(P(X \geq 40) \approx P(Z \geq 2) \approx 0.023\).
  • (c) \(np = 30\) and \(n(1-p) = 150\) are both comfortably above 5.

Week 5 summary

  • \(\bar{X}\) is a random variable: \(E(\bar{X}) = \mu\), \(\text{sd}(\bar{X}) = \sigma/\sqrt{n}\).
  • Normal population → \(\bar{X}\) exactly normal; any population + large \(n\) → approximately normal (CLT).
  • Binomial with large \(n\) ≈ normal with matching mean and variance.
  • Everything in Chapter 16's inference rests on this week's distribution of \(\bar{X}\).
