Key idea

Almost every inequality proof at this level is the same move: bring everything to one side and show the result is a square, a sum of squares, or otherwise visibly non-negative.

Lesson 1 · 2C

The difference method

  • To prove LHS \(\geq\) RHS: show LHS − RHS \(\geq 0\), usually via squares.
  • AM–GM falls out of \((a-b)^2 \geq 0\).
  • Track equality conditions.

Worked example: the difference method

Example 1

Prove that \(a^2 + b^2 \geq 2ab\) for all real \(a, b\), and hence derive the AM–GM inequality \(\dfrac{x+y}{2} \geq \sqrt{xy}\) for \(x, y \geq 0\).

  • Difference: \(a^2 + b^2 - 2ab = (a - b)^2 \geq 0\), with equality iff \(a = b\). ∎
  • Now put \(a = \sqrt{x}\), \(b = \sqrt{y}\) (legal since \(x, y \geq 0\)): \(x + y \geq 2\sqrt{xy}\).
  • Divide by 2: \(\dfrac{x+y}{2} \geq \sqrt{xy}\), equality iff \(x = y\). ∎
Direction of writing

Scratch work may run backwards from the target; the written proof must run forwards from known facts. Assuming the conclusion scores poorly.

Checkpoint 1 — your turn

Try it! (≈6 min)
  • (a) Prove that \(a^2 + b^2 + c^2 \geq ab + bc + ca\) for all real \(a, b, c\). Hint: multiply the difference by 2.
  • (b) When does equality hold?
  • (a) \(2(a^2+b^2+c^2) - 2(ab+bc+ca) = (a-b)^2 + (b-c)^2 + (c-a)^2 \geq 0\). ∎
  • (b) Equality iff every square vanishes: \(a = b = c\).
  • Sum-of-squares is the workhorse: if the difference isn't one square, try to write it as several.
Lesson 2 · 2C

Fractions and surds

  • \(x + \tfrac{1}{x} \geq 2\): the fraction version of the difference method.
  • Conjugates turn surd differences into single fractions you can bound.
  • Check that every hypothesis earns its keep.

Worked example: \(x + \dfrac{1}{x} \geq 2\)

Example 2

Prove that \(x + \dfrac{1}{x} \geq 2\) for all \(x > 0\), with equality only at \(x = 1\).

  • Difference: \(x + \dfrac{1}{x} - 2 = \dfrac{x^2 - 2x + 1}{x} = \dfrac{(x-1)^2}{x}\).
  • For \(x > 0\): numerator \(\geq 0\), denominator \(> 0\), so the difference is \(\geq 0\). ∎
  • Equality iff \((x-1)^2 = 0\), i.e. \(x = 1\).
Hypotheses earn their keep

Where did \(x > 0\) get used? The denominator's sign. If a given condition never appears in your proof, suspect the proof.

Worked example: taming surds

Example 3

Prove that \(\sqrt{n+1} - \sqrt{n} < \dfrac{1}{2\sqrt{n}}\) for all integers \(n \geq 1\).

  • Rationalise: \(\sqrt{n+1} - \sqrt{n} = \dfrac{(\sqrt{n+1} - \sqrt{n})(\sqrt{n+1} + \sqrt{n})}{\sqrt{n+1} + \sqrt{n}} = \dfrac{1}{\sqrt{n+1} + \sqrt{n}}\).
  • Since \(\sqrt{n+1} > \sqrt{n}\), the denominator satisfies \(\sqrt{n+1} + \sqrt{n} > 2\sqrt{n}\).
  • Bigger denominator, smaller fraction: \(\dfrac{1}{\sqrt{n+1} + \sqrt{n}} < \dfrac{1}{2\sqrt{n}}\). ∎
Tool worth naming

Multiplying by the conjugate turns a difference of surds into a single fraction you can bound. This trick reappears in exam proofs constantly.

Checkpoint 2 — your turn

Try it! (≈5 min)

Prove the companion bound: \(\sqrt{n+1} - \sqrt{n} > \dfrac{1}{2\sqrt{n+1}}\) for all integers \(n \geq 1\).

  • Same conjugate step: \(\sqrt{n+1} - \sqrt{n} = \dfrac{1}{\sqrt{n+1} + \sqrt{n}}\).
  • This time bound the other way: \(\sqrt{n+1} + \sqrt{n} < 2\sqrt{n+1}\), so the fraction exceeds \(\dfrac{1}{2\sqrt{n+1}}\). ∎
  • Together the two bounds sandwich \(\sqrt{n+1}-\sqrt{n}\) — the pair is a favourite exam question.
Lesson 3 · 2D

Telescoping series

  • Split the general term as \(f(k) - f(k+1)\); the interior cancels.
  • Products telescope too — match numerators with denominators.
  • Limits fall out of the surviving boundary terms.

Worked example: a sum that collapses

Example 4

Evaluate \(\displaystyle\sum_{k=1}^{n} \frac{1}{k(k+1)}\), and its limit as \(n \to \infty\).

  • Partial fractions: \(\dfrac{1}{k(k+1)} = \dfrac{1}{k} - \dfrac{1}{k+1}\).
  • Write out a few terms: \(\left(1 - \tfrac12\right) + \left(\tfrac12 - \tfrac13\right) + \cdots + \left(\tfrac1n - \tfrac{1}{n+1}\right)\) — the interior cancels in pairs.
  • Survivors: \(1 - \dfrac{1}{n+1} = \dfrac{n}{n+1} \to 1\) as \(n \to \infty\).
Recognising a telescope

If the general term splits as \(f(k) - f(k+1)\) (or \(f(k+1) - f(k)\)), the sum collapses to boundary terms. Partial fractions is the usual splitter.

Seeing it: the interior cancels

Every middle term appears once with \(+\) and once with \(-\). Only the first and last survive.

Products telescope too

Example 5

Evaluate \(\displaystyle\prod_{k=2}^{n} \left(1 - \frac{1}{k^2}\right)\), and its limit as \(n \to \infty\).

  • Factor: \(1 - \dfrac{1}{k^2} = \dfrac{(k-1)(k+1)}{k^2}\), so each numerator meets a matching denominator along the product.
  • Survivors: \(\dfrac{1}{2} \cdot \dfrac{n+1}{n} = \dfrac{n+1}{2n} \to \tfrac12\) as \(n \to \infty\).
Same principle

Sums cancel by subtraction; products cancel by division. Either way, only the boundary survives.

Checkpoint 3 — your turn

Try it! (≈6 min)

Evaluate \(\displaystyle\sum_{k=1}^{n} \frac{1}{k(k+2)}\). Careful: two boundary terms survive at each end.

  • \(\dfrac{1}{k(k+2)} = \dfrac12\left(\dfrac{1}{k} - \dfrac{1}{k+2}\right)\) — the gap of 2 means cancellation skips a term.
  • Survivors: \(\dfrac12\left(1 + \dfrac12 - \dfrac{1}{n+1} - \dfrac{1}{n+2}\right) = \dfrac34 - \dfrac{2n+3}{2(n+1)(n+2)}\).
  • Limit: \(\dfrac34\). Check \(n=1\): formula gives \(\tfrac34 - \tfrac{5}{12} = \tfrac13\) = \(\tfrac{1}{1\cdot3}\). ✓
Lesson 4 · Workshop

Proof-writing workshop

  • The frame, the flow, the finish: setting out arguments for full marks.
  • Diagnose a flawed proof.

Workshop: setting out a proof

  • Declare the frame: "Let \(n \geq 1\) be an integer" / "Suppose, for contradiction, that…".
  • One claim per line, each justified: algebra, a known inequality, or a previously proven line.
  • Direction forwards: known → target. Never begin with the statement to be proved.
  • Close it: state what was shown, note equality conditions if asked, and end with ∎ or "as required".
Marker's-eye view

Assessors award marks for visible logical structure. Two correct proofs can score differently if one hides its reasoning — make every inference inspectable.

Checkpoint 4 — find the flaw

Try it! (≈5 min)

A student "proves" \(a^2 + b^2 \geq 2ab\) like this:

"\(a^2 + b^2 \geq 2ab\). Subtract \(2ab\): \(a^2 - 2ab + b^2 \geq 0\). So \((a-b)^2 \geq 0\), which is true. Hence proved."

The algebra is right and the final line is true. What's wrong — and how should it be written?

  • The argument assumes the conclusion in line 1 and deduces something true — but true conclusions can follow from false premises, so nothing is proved.
  • The steps here are reversible, so the fix is to run them forwards: start from \((a-b)^2 \geq 0\), expand, add \(2ab\). Same algebra, valid direction.
  • Exam habit: scratch backwards, write forwards — and re-read your first line asking "is this known, or is it the target?"

Chapter 2C–D summary

  • Inequalities: difference \(\geq 0\), squares are your witnesses; AM–GM from \((a-b)^2 \geq 0\).
  • Surd bounds: multiply by the conjugate, then bound the denominator.
  • Telescoping: split into \(f(k) - f(k+1)\), keep only the boundary.
  • Written proofs run forwards, one justified claim per line.
