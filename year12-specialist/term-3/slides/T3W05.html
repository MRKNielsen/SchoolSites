Key idea

Estimation asks "what values of \(\mu\) are plausible?" (an interval). Testing asks "is this particular \(\mu_0\) still believable?" (a decision). Same distribution, two questions.

Lesson 1 · 16A

Confidence intervals for the mean

  • Build \(\bar{x} \pm 1.96\,\sigma/\sqrt{n}\) from the distribution of \(\bar{X}\).
  • Say what "95% confident" means — and what it doesn't.
  • Other confidence levels: 90%, 99%.

The 95% confidence interval

\[\left(\bar{x} - 1.96\,\frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}},\;\; \bar{x} + 1.96\,\frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}\right)\]

  • Where does 1.96 come from? \(P(-1.96 < Z < 1.96) = 0.95\) for the standard normal.
  • If \(\sigma\) is unknown and \(n\) is large, use the sample standard deviation \(s\) in its place.
  • Other levels: 90% ↔ 1.645, 99% ↔ 2.576 — wider confidence costs a wider interval.
Example 1

\(n = 36\) batteries last \(\bar{x} = 25\) hours on average; \(\sigma = 6\) hours. Find the 95% CI for \(\mu\).

  • \(\text{sd}(\bar{X}) = \dfrac{6}{\sqrt{36}} = 1\); interval \(= 25 \pm 1.96 \times 1 = (23.04,\; 26.96)\) hours.
Lesson 1 · Confidence intervalsVCE Specialist Mathematics 3&4

What "95% confident" actually means

  • The interval is random; \(\mu\) is fixed. Before sampling, there is a 95% chance the interval we will construct captures \(\mu\).
  • Correct sentence: "If we repeated this sampling process many times, about 95% of the intervals produced would contain \(\mu\)."
  • Not correct: "There is a 95% probability that \(\mu\) lies in \((23.04, 26.96)\)" — once computed, the interval either contains \(\mu\) or it doesn't.
Width levers

Width \(= 2z\dfrac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}\). To halve the width: quadruple \(n\), or accept lower confidence. Exam questions love "find \(n\) so the width is at most …".

Twenty samples, twenty intervals

Each sample of \(n=36\) gives its own interval \(\bar{x} \pm 1.96\); in the long run about 1 in 20 miss.

Try it! (≈5 min)

A sample of \(n = 64\) test scores gives \(\bar{x} = 102\); the population sd is \(\sigma = 16\).

  • (a) Find the 95% confidence interval for \(\mu\).
  • (b) Find the 90% interval. Which is wider, and why?
  • (a) \(\text{sd}(\bar{X}) = \dfrac{16}{8} = 2\); \(102 \pm 1.96(2) = (98.08,\; 105.92)\).
  • (b) \(102 \pm 1.645(2) = (98.71,\; 105.29)\) — narrower: less confidence buys a tighter interval.

Lesson 2 · 16A

Width and sample size

  • Width \(= 2z\sigma/\sqrt{n}\): solve for \(n\) given a target width.
  • Round the right way, and respect the square-root law.

Worked example: choosing the sample size

Example 2

Battery lifetimes have \(\sigma = 6\) hours. How many batteries must we test so that a 95% confidence interval for \(\mu\) has width at most 2 hours?

  • Width \(= 2 \times 1.96\,\dfrac{6}{\sqrt{n}} \leq 2 \;\Longleftrightarrow\; \sqrt{n} \geq 1.96 \times 6 = 11.76\).
  • \(n \geq 138.3\), and \(n\) is a whole number of batteries: \(n = 139\).
Round the right way

Always round up — 138 batteries gives an interval slightly too wide. And note the square-root law: halving the width quadruples the sample.

Try it! (≈5 min)

Delivery times have \(\sigma = 12\) minutes.

  • (a) How large a sample is needed for a 99% CI of width at most 4 minutes?
  • (b) How does the answer change at 95% confidence?
  • (a) \(2 \times 2.576 \times \dfrac{12}{\sqrt{n}} \leq 4 \Rightarrow \sqrt{n} \geq 15.456 \Rightarrow n \geq 238.9\): \(n = 239\).
  • (b) \(\sqrt{n} \geq 1.96 \times 6 = 11.76 \Rightarrow n = 139\) — a hundred fewer: high confidence is expensive.

Lesson 3 · 16B

The logic of a hypothesis test

  • \(H_0\) is the status quo; \(H_1\) is the suspicion — fixed before seeing data.
  • The \(p\)-value: how surprising is our sample, if \(H_0\) is true?
  • The decision rule, and why "do not reject" ≠ "accept".

The logic of a hypothesis test

  • Null hypothesis \(H_0: \mu = \mu_0\) — the status quo, "nothing has changed".
  • Alternative \(H_1\) — what we suspect: \(\mu < \mu_0\), \(\mu > \mu_0\) or \(\mu \neq \mu_0\).
  • Strategy: assume \(H_0\), then measure how surprising the data would be.
p-value

The probability, assuming \(H_0\) is true, of observing a sample mean at least as extreme as the one we got.

Decision rule

Small \(p\)-value (below the significance level, usually \(\alpha = 0.05\)) → the data are too surprising for \(H_0\) → reject \(H_0\). Otherwise: do not reject \(H_0\) — which is not the same as proving it true.

Worked example: a full test

Example 3

Recovery takes 5 days on average with the current drug (\(\sigma = 1\) day). On a new drug, 25 patients average \(\bar{x} = 4.6\) days. Test at the 5% level whether recovery time has reduced (\(\sigma\) unchanged).

  • \(H_0: \mu = 5\); \(H_1: \mu < 5\) (one-tail — we're asking about a reduction).
  • Under \(H_0\): \(\bar{X} \sim N\!\left(5,\, \left(\tfrac{1}{5}\right)^2\right)\). Test statistic: \(z = \dfrac{4.6 - 5}{1/\sqrt{25}} = -2\).
  • \(p = P(Z \leq -2) \approx 0.0228 < 0.05\): reject \(H_0\) — evidence, at the 5% level, that the new drug reduces mean recovery time.
Write the conclusion in context

Always end with a sentence about drugs and days — not just "reject \(H_0\)".

Lesson 3 · Hypothesis testingVCE Specialist Mathematics 3&4

Seeing it: the \(p\)-value is a tail area

Under \(H_0\), \(\bar{X} \sim N(5,\, 0.2^2)\). Our \(\bar{x} = 4.6\) sits at \(z = -2\); the shaded tail (0.0228) is the \(p\)-value.

Lesson 4 · 16B

Full tests, written properly

  • Run complete tests start to finish, both directions of \(H_1\).
  • Practise the five-line setting-out that earns full marks.

The five-line setting-out

  1. Hypotheses: \(H_0: \mu = \mu_0\); \(H_1\) with direction justified by the question's wording.
  2. Distribution under \(H_0\): \(\bar{X} \sim N(\mu_0,\, \sigma^2/n)\) — cite normal population or CLT.
  3. Test statistic: \(z = \dfrac{\bar{x} - \mu_0}{\sigma/\sqrt{n}}\) with the numbers shown.
  4. p-value: the correct tail probability, compared against \(\alpha\).
  5. Conclusion in context: reject / do not reject, then a sentence with units.
Marker's-eye view

Each line carries marks independently. A correct \(p\)-value after wrong hypotheses still loses the setup marks — write line 1 before touching the CAS.

Worked example: testing an increase

Example 4

A machine fills bags with \(\mu = 500\) g of flour, \(\sigma = 8\) g. After maintenance, a sample of 16 bags has \(\bar{x} = 504.5\) g. At the 5% level, is there evidence the machine now overfills?

  • \(H_0: \mu = 500\); \(H_1: \mu > 500\) ("overfills" → one-tail, upper).
  • \(z = \dfrac{504.5 - 500}{8/\sqrt{16}} = \dfrac{4.5}{2} = 2.25\).
  • \(p = P(Z \geq 2.25) \approx 0.0122 < 0.05\) → reject \(H_0\): evidence, at the 5% level, that the machine now overfills bags.

Checkpoint 4 — your turn

Try it! (≈6 min)

Cereal boxes should contain \(\mu = 750\) g with \(\sigma = 10\) g. Consumer-watch suspects underfilling. A sample of 25 boxes gives \(\bar{x} = 745.2\) g. Test at the 1% level, writing all five lines.

  • \(H_0: \mu = 750\); \(H_1: \mu < 750\). Under \(H_0\): \(\bar{X} \sim N(750,\, 2^2)\).
  • \(z = \dfrac{745.2 - 750}{10/5} = -2.4\); \(p = P(Z \leq -2.4) \approx 0.0082\).
  • \(p < 0.01\) → reject \(H_0\): evidence, at the 1% level, that boxes are being underfilled on average.

Week 5 summary

  • 95% CI: \(\bar{x} \pm 1.96\sigma/\sqrt{n}\); interpret via repeated sampling, and know the width levers.
  • Tests: state \(H_0\) and \(H_1\) before touching the data; compute \(z\); convert to a \(p\)-value.
  • \(p < \alpha\) → reject \(H_0\); otherwise "do not reject" — never "accept".
  • Conclusions live in context: days, grams, hours — not just symbols.
