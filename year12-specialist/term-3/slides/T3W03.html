Key idea

Means combine exactly as you'd hope. Variances don't: they pick up squared coefficients, and they add even when you subtract. Most marks lost this term trace back to that one sentence.

Lesson 1 · 15A

Linear functions of a random variable

  • \(E(aX+b) = aE(X) + b\) — means follow the algebra.
  • \(\text{Var}(aX+b) = a^2\text{Var}(X)\) — shifts vanish, scales square.
  • Apply to unit changes, taxes, fees and other linear stories.

Scaling and shifting: \(Y = aX + b\)

\[E(aX + b) = a\,E(X) + b \qquad\quad \text{Var}(aX + b) = a^2\,\text{Var}(X) \qquad\quad \text{sd}(aX+b) = |a|\,\text{sd}(X)\]

  • Shifting by \(b\) moves the centre but does nothing to the spread.
  • Scaling by \(a\) stretches the spread by \(|a|\) — so the variance stretches by \(a^2\).
Example 1

\(X\) has distribution \(P(X=0)=0.3\), \(P(X=1)=0.4\), \(P(X=2)=0.3\). Find \(E(Y)\) and \(\text{Var}(Y)\) for \(Y = 3X + 2\).

  • \(E(X) = 0(0.3) + 1(0.4) + 2(0.3) = 1\); \(E(X^2) = 0 + 0.4 + 4(0.3) = 1.6\), so \(\text{Var}(X) = 1.6 - 1 = 0.6\).
  • \(E(Y) = 3(1) + 2 = 5\); \(\text{Var}(Y) = 3^2(0.6) = 5.4\). The \(+2\) never touches the variance.

Worked example: a change of units

Example 2

Daily maximum temperatures in January have mean \(25\,^{\circ}\)C and standard deviation \(2\,^{\circ}\)C. A US weather app reports \(F = 1.8C + 32\). Find the mean and standard deviation in \(^{\circ}\)F.

  • \(E(F) = 1.8(25) + 32 = 77\,^{\circ}\)F.
  • \(\text{sd}(F) = |1.8| \times 2 = 3.6\,^{\circ}\)F — the \(+32\) does not touch the spread.
  • Variance if you need it: \(\text{Var}(F) = 1.8^2 \times 4 = 12.96\) — never \(1.8 \times 4\).
Workflow

Write the linear relationship first, then read off \(a\) and \(b\). The formulas do the rest.

Lesson 2 · 15B

Linear combinations of independent variables

  • \(E(aX+bY) = aE(X) + bE(Y)\) — always.
  • \(\text{Var}(aX+bY) = a^2\text{Var}(X) + b^2\text{Var}(Y)\) — needs independence, and it always adds.
  • The classic distinction: \(2X\) versus \(X_1 + X_2\).

Combining two variables: \(aX + bY\)

\[E(aX + bY) = a\,E(X) + b\,E(Y)\]

\[\text{Var}(aX + bY) = a^2\,\text{Var}(X) + b^2\,\text{Var}(Y) \quad \text{(for independent } X, Y\text{)}\]

The subtraction trap

\(\text{Var}(X - Y) = \text{Var}(X) + \text{Var}(Y)\). Differences are just as variable as sums — the \((-1)^2\) kills the minus sign. Variances of independent variables never subtract.

Requirement

The variance rule needs independence. The expectation rule doesn't — it always holds.

The classic distinction: \(2X\) versus \(X_1 + X_2\)

2X — one measurement, doubled

\(E(2X) = 2\mu\)

\(\text{Var}(2X) = 4\sigma^2\)

X₁ + X₂ — two independent copies

\(E(X_1 + X_2) = 2\mu\)

\(\text{Var}(X_1 + X_2) = 2\sigma^2\)

  • Same mean, different spread: two independent draws partly cancel each other's fluctuations; doubling one draw doubles its error.
  • Exam wording: "the total weight of two randomly chosen boxes" is \(X_1 + X_2\); "twice the weight of a randomly chosen box" is \(2X\).
Discuss

Generalise: \(\text{Var}(X_1 + \cdots + X_n) = n\sigma^2\), but \(\text{Var}(nX) = n^2\sigma^2\). Which grows faster, and why does that make sense?

Try it! (≈5 min)

\(X\) has mean 50 and variance 9; \(Y\) has mean 20 and variance 4; \(X\) and \(Y\) are independent.

  • (a) Find the mean and variance of \(3X - 2Y\).

Lesson 3 · 15C

Linear combinations of normal variables

  • Independent normals combine to a normal — with mean and variance from the rules.
  • The four-step workflow: name it → mean & variance → state the distribution → standardise.
  • Differences of normals: who-beats-whom problems.

Normality survives linear combination

Fact

If \(X\) and \(Y\) are independent normals, then \(aX + bY + c\) is also normal — mean and variance from the rules above.

Example 3

Juice bottles: \(X \sim N(502,\, 2^2)\) mL. Find \(P(\text{four-pack total} < 2000)\).

  • Name it: \(T = X_1 + X_2 + X_3 + X_4\) (four independent bottles, not \(4X\)).
  • \(E(T) = 4 \times 502 = 2008\); \(\text{Var}(T) = 4 \times 4 = 16\); so \(T \sim N(2008,\, 4^2)\).
  • \(P(T < 2000) = P\left(Z < \dfrac{2000 - 2008}{4}\right) = P(Z < -2) \approx 0.0228\).
All four steps carry marks

Name the combination → mean & variance → state the distribution → standardise.

Worked example: comparing two normals

Example 4

Tom's swim times are \(N(62,\, 1.5^2)\) s; Sam's are \(N(60,\, 2^2)\) s, independently. Find the probability Tom beats Sam in a race.

  • Tom wins when \(T < S\), i.e. \(D = T - S < 0\).
  • \(E(D) = 62 - 60 = 2\); \(\text{Var}(D) = 1.5^2 + 2^2 = 6.25\), so \(D \sim N(2,\, 2.5^2)\).
  • \(P(D < 0) = P\left(Z < \dfrac{0 - 2}{2.5}\right) = P(Z < -0.8) \approx 0.212\).
Note the variance

Even though we subtracted the means, we added the variances: \(1.5^2 + 2^2\), not \(2^2 - 1.5^2\).

Lesson 3 · Normal combinationsVCE Specialist Mathematics 3&4

Seeing it: the difference is wider than either

\(D = T - S\) has sd \(2.5\) — larger than Tom's \(1.5\) and Sam's \(2\). Subtracting added uncertainty. Tom wins on the shaded side \(D < 0\).

Checkpoint 3 — your turn

Try it! (≈5 min)

Bottles are filled with \(X \sim N(502,\, 2^2)\) mL, independently.

  • (a) Find the probability one bottle contains less than 500 mL.
  • (b) Find the probability a six-pack's total is less than 3000 mL.
  • (a) \(P(X < 500) = P\!\left(Z < \dfrac{-2}{2}\right) = P(Z < -1) \approx 0.159\).
  • (b) \(T \sim N(3012,\, 24)\), sd \(= \sqrt{24} \approx 4.90\); \(P(T < 3000) = P(Z < -2.449) \approx 0.007\).
  • One bottle is short 16% of the time; a whole six-pack almost never is — independent errors partly cancel.

Worked example: a full combination problem

Example 5

A latte is one espresso shot \(X \sim N(30,\, 2^2)\) mL plus steamed milk \(M \sim N(180,\, 5^2)\) mL, independent. The cup holds 220 mL. Find the probability of overflow.

  • \(L = X + M\): \(E(L) = 210\); \(\text{Var}(L) = 4 + 25 = 29\); so \(L \sim N(210,\, 29)\), sd \(= \sqrt{29} \approx 5.39\).
  • \(P(L > 220) = P\!\left(Z > \dfrac{10}{\sqrt{29}}\right) = P(Z > 1.857) \approx 0.032\).
  • About 1 latte in 31 overflows. (What could the café change: \(\mu\) of the milk, or its \(\sigma\)? Both work — which is cheaper?)
