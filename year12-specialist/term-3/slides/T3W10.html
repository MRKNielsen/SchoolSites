Key idea

Dominoes: prove the first one falls (base case), and that each falling domino knocks over the next (inductive step). Then they all fall.

Lesson 1 · 2E

The principle of mathematical induction

  • Base case + inductive step = infinitely many statements proved.
  • The framework, written in full, every time.
  • What induction is for — and where the base case starts.

The template

Principle

To prove \(P(n)\) for all integers \(n \geq n_0\):   (1) Base case: verify \(P(n_0)\).   (2) Inductive step: assume \(P(k)\) for some \(k \geq n_0\), and deduce \(P(k+1)\).

  • The assumption in step 2 is the inductive hypothesis — you must visibly use it.
  • Write the framework every time: base case, hypothesis, step, conclusion "by induction, \(P(n)\) holds for all \(n \geq n_0\)".
What induction is for

Claims indexed by integers: sum/product formulas, divisibility facts, inequalities like \(2^n > n^2\) for \(n \geq 5\).

Seeing it: dominoes

Base case: \(P(1)\) falls. Inductive step: each \(P(k)\) knocks over \(P(k+1)\). Conclusion: they all fall.

Checkpoint 1 — your turn

Try it! (≈4 min)
  • (a) For the claim "\(2^n > n^2\) for all \(n \geq 5\)", what exactly must the base case verify?
  • (b) A student proves the inductive step perfectly but skips the base case. What can go wrong? Find a claim where the step works but the claim is false.
  • (a) \(n = 5\): \(2^5 = 32 > 25 = 5^2\). ✓ (Not \(n = 1\) — the claim starts at 5, and indeed fails at \(n = 2, 3, 4\).)
  • (b) Example: "\(n = n + 1\) for all \(n\)". If \(k = k+1\) then adding 1 gives \(k+1 = k+2\) — a flawless step! But no domino ever falls: there's no base case.
Lesson 2 · 2E

Induction on sums

  • The move: add the \((m+1)\)-th term into the hypothesis, then massage.
  • Factor early — aim the algebra at the target formula.

Worked example: a sum formula

Example 1

Prove \(\displaystyle\sum_{k=1}^{n} k^2 = \frac{n(n+1)(2n+1)}{6}\) for all \(n \geq 1\).

  • Base: \(n=1\): LHS \(= 1\), RHS \(= \dfrac{1 \cdot 2 \cdot 3}{6} = 1\). ✓
  • Hypothesis: assume \(\displaystyle\sum_{k=1}^{m} k^2 = \frac{m(m+1)(2m+1)}{6}\) for some \(m \geq 1\).
  • Step: \(\displaystyle\sum_{k=1}^{m+1} k^2 = \frac{m(m+1)(2m+1)}{6} + (m+1)^2 = \frac{(m+1)\big(m(2m+1) + 6(m+1)\big)}{6}\) — factor \((m+1)\) out early.
  • \(m(2m+1) + 6(m+1) = 2m^2+7m+6 = (m+2)(2m+3)\) — exactly the formula with \(n = m+1\). Conclude by induction. ∎

Checkpoint 2 — your turn

Try it! (≈6 min)

Prove by induction: \(\displaystyle\sum_{k=1}^{n} k = \frac{n(n+1)}{2}\) for all \(n \geq 1\). Write all four framework lines.

  • Base: \(n=1\): LHS \(= 1 =\) RHS \(= \tfrac{1\cdot2}{2}\). ✓   Hypothesis: \(\sum_{k=1}^{m} k = \tfrac{m(m+1)}{2}\).
  • Step: \(\displaystyle\sum_{k=1}^{m+1} k = \frac{m(m+1)}{2} + (m+1) = \frac{(m+1)(m+2)}{2}\) — the formula at \(n = m+1\).
  • Conclusion: by the principle of mathematical induction, the formula holds for all \(n \geq 1\). ∎
Lesson 3 · 2E

Induction on divisibility

  • The pivot: solve the hypothesis for the power, substitute, factor.
  • Every divisibility induction runs on the same rails.

Worked example: divisibility

Example 2

Prove that \(11^n - 6\) is divisible by 5 for all integers \(n \geq 1\).

  • Base: \(n = 1\): \(11 - 6 = 5\). ✓
  • Hypothesis: \(11^m - 6 = 5j\) for some integer \(j\) — solve for the power: \(11^m = 5j + 6\).
  • Step: \(11^{m+1} - 6 = 11 \cdot 11^m - 6 = 11(5j + 6) - 6 = 55j + 60 = 5(11j + 12)\) — divisible by 5. ∎
The move

Divisibility inductions all pivot the same way: solve the hypothesis for the power, substitute, and factor the base out of what remains.

Checkpoint 3 — your turn

Try it! (≈6 min)

Prove by induction: \(7^n - 1\) is divisible by 6 for all integers \(n \geq 1\).

  • Base: \(n=1\): \(7 - 1 = 6\). ✓   Hypothesis: \(7^m - 1 = 6j\), i.e. \(7^m = 6j + 1\).
  • Step: \(7^{m+1} - 1 = 7(6j + 1) - 1 = 42j + 6 = 6(7j + 1)\). ∎
  • Same pivot, new numbers. Say where the hypothesis entered — that's the mark that distinguishes induction from wishful thinking.
Lesson 4 · 2E

Products, inequalities — and the wrap-up

  • Products: multiply the next factor into the hypothesis.
  • Inequality inductions: one honest bound closes the step.
  • Where induction proofs lose marks; the holiday plan.

Worked example: a product

Example 3

Prove \(\displaystyle\prod_{k=2}^{n} \left(1 - \frac{1}{k}\right) = \frac{1}{n}\) for all \(n \geq 2\).

  • Base: \(n = 2\): LHS \(= 1 - \tfrac12 = \tfrac12\) = RHS. ✓
  • Step: \(\displaystyle\prod_{k=2}^{m+1}\left(1 - \frac{1}{k}\right) = \frac{1}{m}\left(1 - \frac{1}{m+1}\right) = \frac{1}{m} \cdot \frac{m}{m+1} = \frac{1}{m+1}\). ∎
  • Telescoping (last week) finds the formula; induction proves it. Two tools, one truth.

Worked example: an inequality

Example 4

Prove that \(2^n > n^2\) for all integers \(n \geq 5\).

  • Base: \(n = 5\): \(32 > 25\). ✓
  • Hypothesis: \(2^m > m^2\) for some \(m \geq 5\). Step: \(2^{m+1} = 2 \cdot 2^m > 2m^2\).
  • Enough to show \(2m^2 \geq (m+1)^2\): difference \(= m^2 - 2m - 1 = (m-1)^2 - 2 > 0\) for \(m \geq 5\). ∎
  • Pattern: hypothesis gives the first bound; a small side-inequality (proved honestly, using \(m \geq n_0\)) closes the gap.

Where induction proofs lose marks

  • No base case — or the wrong one (start where the claim starts, e.g. \(n = 5\) for \(2^n > n^2\)).
  • Hypothesis never used — if your step doesn't invoke \(P(k)\), it isn't induction.
  • Assuming \(P(k+1)\) — working backwards from what you're meant to prove.
  • Missing conclusion — finish with "by the principle of mathematical induction, … for all \(n \geq n_0\)."
Self-check

Underline where the inductive hypothesis enters your step. Can't find it? The proof is broken.
