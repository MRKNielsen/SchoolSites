Key idea

A proof is an argument that leaves no escape route. The technique you pick — direct, contrapositive, contradiction — is just a choice of which direction to close the exits from.

Lesson 1 · 2A

Conditionals and direct proof

  • \(P \Rightarrow Q\), its converse and its contrapositive — which are equivalent?
  • "Iff" means two proofs.
  • Direct proof: name the form, do the algebra, conclude.

Conditional statements and their relatives

StatementFormTied to original?
Conditional\(P \Rightarrow Q\)—
Converse\(Q \Rightarrow P\)No — independent
Contrapositive\(\neg Q \Rightarrow \neg P\)Yes — equivalent
  • \(P \Leftrightarrow Q\) ("if and only if") requires proving both directions.
  • "\(n\) even \(\Rightarrow n^2\) even" is true; its converse also happens to be true — but that needed its own proof.

Worked example: direct proof

Example 1

Prove that the sum of two odd integers is even.

  • Name the form: let the integers be \(2m+1\) and \(2n+1\) for integers \(m, n\) — this is what "odd" means.
  • Algebra: \((2m+1) + (2n+1) = 2m + 2n + 2 = 2(m+n+1)\).
  • Conclude: \(m+n+1\) is an integer, so the sum is twice an integer — even. ∎
The rhythm of direct proof

Translate the hypothesis into algebraic form → manipulate → recognise the conclusion's form. Every definition you write down is a mark.

Checkpoint 1 — your turn

Try it! (≈5 min)
  • (a) Prove directly: the product of two odd integers is odd.
  • (b) State the converse and the contrapositive of: "if it is raining, the ground is wet." Which is logically equivalent to the original?
  • (a) \((2m+1)(2n+1) = 4mn + 2m + 2n + 1 = 2(2mn + m + n) + 1\) — odd. ∎
  • (b) Converse: "if the ground is wet, it is raining" (not equivalent — sprinklers exist). Contrapositive: "if the ground is not wet, it is not raining" — equivalent.
Lesson 2 · 2A

Proof by contrapositive

  • When the hypothesis is hard to grip, prove \(\neg Q \Rightarrow \neg P\) instead.
  • Splitting into exhaustive cases is legitimate — say why they cover everything.

Worked example: proof by contrapositive

Example 2

Prove: if \(n^2\) is even, then \(n\) is even.

  • Direct attack is awkward — "\(n^2\) even" is hard to use. Contrapositive: if \(n\) is odd, then \(n^2\) is odd.
  • \(n = 2k+1 \Rightarrow n^2 = 4k^2 + 4k + 1 = 2(2k^2 + 2k) + 1\) — odd. ∎
  • Since the contrapositive is equivalent, the original is proved.
When to reach for it

When the hypothesis is a "negative-flavoured" or hard-to-manipulate condition and its negation is concrete. "Not even" becomes "odd = \(2k+1\)" — algebra you can touch.

Worked example: contrapositive with cases

Example 3

Prove: if \(3\) divides \(n^2\), then \(3\) divides \(n\).

  • Contrapositive: if \(3 \nmid n\), then \(3 \nmid n^2\). Now "not divisible" is concrete: \(n = 3k+1\) or \(n = 3k+2\).
  • Case 1: \(n = 3k+1\): \(n^2 = 3(3k^2 + 2k) + 1\).   Case 2: \(n = 3k+2\): \(n^2 = 3(3k^2 + 4k + 1) + 1\). Remainder 1 both times.
  • Either way \(3 \nmid n^2\). ∎   Exhaustive cases are a legitimate technique — just say why they cover everything. (This is the engine inside the \(\sqrt{3}\)-irrational proof.)

Checkpoint 2 — your turn

Try it! (≈5 min)
  • (a) Prove by contrapositive: if \(n^2\) is odd, then \(n\) is odd.
  • (b) Prove by contrapositive: if \(5n + 3\) is even, then \(n\) is odd.
  • (a) Contrapositive: \(n\) even \(\Rightarrow n^2\) even. \(n = 2k \Rightarrow n^2 = 2(2k^2)\). ∎
  • (b) Contrapositive: \(n\) even \(\Rightarrow 5n+3\) odd. \(n = 2k \Rightarrow 5n + 3 = 10k + 3 = 2(5k+1) + 1\) — odd. ∎
Lesson 3 · 2A

Proof by contradiction

  • Negate precisely → deduce honestly → hit an impossibility.
  • The classic: \(\sqrt{2}\) is irrational.

Proof by contradiction: \(\sqrt{2}\) is irrational

  • Suppose not: \(\sqrt{2} = \dfrac{p}{q}\) with \(p, q\) integers sharing no common factor.
  • Then \(p^2 = 2q^2\): \(p^2\) even, so \(p\) even (Lesson 2's theorem!) — write \(p = 2m\).
  • Substitute: \(4m^2 = 2q^2 \Rightarrow q^2 = 2m^2\), so \(q\) is even too.
  • Now \(p\) and \(q\) share the factor 2 — contradicting our "no common factor" setup. So no such fraction exists. ∎
Anatomy

Negate precisely → deduce honestly → hit an impossibility → conclude. Note how the contrapositive result from Lesson 2 was a lemma here — proofs stack.

Checkpoint 3 — your turn

Try it! (≈6 min)
  • (a) Prove by contradiction: there is no largest integer.
  • (b) Prove by contradiction: there is no smallest positive rational number.
  • (a) Suppose \(N\) is the largest integer. Then \(N + 1\) is an integer and \(N + 1 > N\) — contradiction. ∎
  • (b) Suppose \(q\) is the smallest positive rational. Then \(\tfrac{q}{2}\) is rational, positive, and smaller — contradiction. ∎
  • Both proofs manufacture a better witness from the assumed extreme one — a standard contradiction pattern.
Lesson 4 · 2B

Quantifiers and counterexamples

  • \(\forall\) and \(\exists\): what each demands of a proof.
  • Negation swaps the quantifier.
  • One counterexample kills a universal claim.

For all, there exists

  • \(\forall\): "for all" — a universal claim. \(\forall x \in \mathbb{R},\; x^2 \geq 0\).
  • \(\exists\): "there exists" — an existence claim. \(\exists n \in \mathbb{Z},\; n^2 = 49\).
  • Order matters: \(\forall x\, \exists y\, (y > x)\) is true in \(\mathbb{R}\); \(\exists y\, \forall x\, (y > x)\) is false.
Negation rules

\(\neg\big(\forall x, P(x)\big) \equiv \exists x, \neg P(x)\)

\(\neg\big(\exists x, P(x)\big) \equiv \forall x, \neg P(x)\)

Negation swaps the quantifier.

Proof consequences

To prove \(\forall\): argue for an arbitrary element. To disprove \(\forall\): exhibit one counterexample. To prove \(\exists\): exhibit one witness. To disprove \(\exists\): argue for all.

Disproof by counterexample

Example 4

Claim: "for all primes \(p\), \(2^p - 1\) is prime." Disprove it.

  • Test small primes: \(p = 2, 3, 5, 7\) give \(3, 7, 31, 127\) — all prime. Suspicious streak…
  • \(p = 11\): \(2^{11} - 1 = 2047 = 23 \times 89\). One counterexample; claim dead. ∎
  • Check both parts explicitly: \(11\) is prime (hypothesis ✓), \(2047\) is composite (conclusion ✗).
Discipline

A counterexample must satisfy the hypothesis and violate the conclusion — check both, every time. And remember: examples can disprove a universal claim, never prove one.

Checkpoint 4 — your turn

Try it! (≈5 min)
  • (a) Disprove: "every prime is odd."
  • (b) Disprove: "for all real \(x\), \(x^2 > x\)."
  • (c) Write the negation of: "\(\exists y\) such that \(\forall x\), \(y > x\)." Is the negation true in \(\mathbb{R}\)?
  • (a) \(p = 2\): prime and even. ∎   (b) \(x = \tfrac12\): \(\tfrac14 < \tfrac12\) (or \(x = 0\): \(0 \not> 0\)). ∎
  • (c) "\(\forall y\, \exists x\) with \(x \geq y\)" — true: given any \(y\), take \(x = y\) (or \(y+1\)). No largest real exists.

Week 8 summary

  • Contrapositive is equivalent; converse is not. "Iff" needs both directions.
  • Contradiction: negate precisely, push to impossibility.
  • Quantifier negation swaps \(\forall \leftrightarrow \exists\); proof obligations follow the quantifier.
  • One counterexample kills a universal claim; no number of examples proves one.
