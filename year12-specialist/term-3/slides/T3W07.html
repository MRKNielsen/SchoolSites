Formula board

Combinations

\(E(aX + bY) = aE(X) + bE(Y)\)

\(\text{Var}(aX + bY) = a^2\text{Var}(X) + b^2\text{Var}(Y)\)

\(\text{sd} = \sqrt{\text{Var}}\) — convert at the last step

Sample mean

\(E(\bar{X}) = \mu\), \(\text{sd}(\bar{X}) = \dfrac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}\)

Inference

CI: \(\bar{x} \pm z\dfrac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}\)   (95%: \(z = 1.96\))

Test: \(z = \dfrac{\bar{x} - \mu_0}{\sigma/\sqrt{n}}\)

two-tail: \(p = 2P(Z \geq |z|)\)

Binomial → normal

\(\text{Bi}(n,p) \mathrel{\dot\sim} N\big(np,\, np(1-p)\big)\), large \(n\)

SAC-style question 1: combinations

Question

A gift box holds 4 chocolates and 2 truffles. Chocolate masses are \(N(20,\, 1.5^2)\) g; truffle masses are \(N(35,\, 2^2)\) g; the empty box is exactly 50 g. Find the distribution of the total mass, and \(P(\text{total} > 205)\) g.

  • \(T = C_1 + \cdots + C_4 + R_1 + R_2 + 50\) — six independent copies, not \(4C + 2R\).
  • \(E(T) = 4(20) + 2(35) + 50 = 200\);   \(\text{Var}(T) = 4(1.5^2) + 2(2^2) = 17\) (the 50 adds no variance).
  • \(T \sim N(200,\, 17)\); \(P(T > 205) = P\!\left(Z > \tfrac{5}{\sqrt{17}}\right) = P(Z > 1.213) \approx 0.113\).
Traps dodged

Six independent copies — not \(4C + 2R\); constants shift means only.

SAC-style question 2: sample mean

Question

A café's espresso shots have mean 30 mL and sd 3 mL (any shape of distribution). For a day of \(n = 81\) shots, estimate the probability the day's average shot exceeds 30.5 mL.

  • Population not stated normal — but \(n = 81\) is large, so by the CLT \(\bar{X} \mathrel{\dot\sim} N\!\left(30,\, \left(\tfrac{3}{9}\right)^2\right) = N(30,\, \tfrac{1}{9})\).
  • \(P(\bar{X} > 30.5) \approx P\!\left(Z > \dfrac{0.5}{1/3}\right) = P(Z > 1.5) \approx 0.0668\).
  • Cite the CLT explicitly — naming the theorem is part of the answer.

SAC-style question 3: full hypothesis test

Question

Bags of coffee should average 250 g with \(\sigma = 4\) g. A random sample of 20 bags (population normal) gives \(\bar{x} = 247.8\) g. Test at the 5% level whether the mean has changed, and state the corresponding 95% CI.

  • \(H_0: \mu = 250\), \(H_1: \mu \neq 250\) ("changed" → two-tail).
  • \(z = \dfrac{247.8 - 250}{4/\sqrt{20}} = \dfrac{-2.2}{0.894} \approx -2.46\); \(p = 2P(Z \geq 2.46) \approx 0.0139\).
  • \(p < 0.05\) → reject \(H_0\): evidence the filling mean has changed.
  • CI: \(247.8 \pm 1.96(0.894) = (246.0,\, 249.6)\) — and indeed 250 lies outside it. Consistent.
Lesson 3 · Revision

Mark-losers and rapid fire

  • The four classic traps, named and disarmed.
  • Rapid-fire drill: ten quick answers under time.

The four classic mark-losers

  • Variance vs sd: combining sds directly. Always combine variances, square-root at the end.
  • \(2X\) vs \(X_1 + X_2\): "twice one item" vs "two random items". Var \(4\sigma^2\) vs \(2\sigma^2\).
  • Tail choice: decided by the question's wording, before seeing the data. "Changed" = two-tail.
  • Conclusion language: "do not reject \(H_0\)" (never "accept \(H_0\)"), and always a final sentence in context with units.
Self-test

Can you write, from memory, the distribution of \(\bar{X}\) for a sample of \(n\) from \(N(\mu, \sigma^2)\) — and say what changes if the population isn't normal?

Rapid fire — 6 in 6 minutes

Try it! (1 min each)
  • 1. \(X\): mean 8, sd 3. Mean and sd of \(5 - 2X\)?
  • 2. \(\text{Var}(X - Y)\) for independent \(X, Y\) with variances 5 and 7?
  • 3. sd of \(\bar{X}\), sample of 25 from sd-10 population?
  • 4. 95% CI: \(n = 100\), \(\bar{x} = 52\), \(\sigma = 5\)?
  • 5. "Has the mean increased?" — which tail?
  • 6. \(P(\text{Type I error})\) at significance level 0.05?
  • 1. mean \(5 - 16 = -11\); sd \(|-2| \times 3 = 6\).   2. \(5 + 7 = 12\).   3. \(10/5 = 2\).
  • 4. \(52 \pm 0.98 = (51.02,\, 52.98)\).   5. upper one-tail (\(H_1: \mu > \mu_0\)).   6. \(0.05 = \alpha\), by definition.
