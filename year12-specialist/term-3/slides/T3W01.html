Lesson 1 · 12A

Position, velocity and acceleration

  • The three functions of motion, and the derivatives that link them.
  • Displacement vs distance travelled — and why they differ at every turnaround.
  • Read a full journey from \(x(t)\).

The three functions of motion

  • Position \(x(t)\): coordinate on the line, signed relative to the origin \(O\).
  • Velocity \(v = \dfrac{dx}{dt}\): rate of change of position. Signed.
  • Acceleration \(a = \dfrac{dv}{dt} = \dfrac{d^2x}{dt^2}\): rate of change of velocity.
Watch the vocabulary

Displacement = change in position (signed). Distance travelled = total ground covered (never negative). They differ whenever the particle turns around.

Averages vs instants

Average velocity \(= \dfrac{x(t_2)-x(t_1)}{t_2-t_1}\)

Average speed \(= \dfrac{\text{distance travelled}}{t_2-t_1}\)

Instantaneous versions are the derivatives.

Worked example: reading a journey from \(x(t)\)

Example 1

A particle moves so that \(x(t) = t^3 - 6t^2 + 9t\) metres after \(t\) seconds. Find when it is at rest, and the distance travelled in the first four seconds.

  • \(v(t) = 3t^2 - 12t + 9 = 3(t-1)(t-3)\) — at rest when \(t = 1\) and \(t = 3\).
  • Positions at the turnarounds and endpoints: \(x(0)=0\), \(x(1)=4\), \(x(3)=0\), \(x(4)=4\).
  • The particle goes \(0 \to 4 \to 0 \to 4\): distance \(= 4 + 4 + 4 = 12\) m, but displacement is only \(4\) m.
  • So average speed \(= \dfrac{12}{4} = 3\) m/s, while average velocity \(= \dfrac{4}{4} = 1\) m/s.
Method

Distance questions: find every time \(v = 0\) in the interval, evaluate \(x\) at each turning time and the endpoints, then add the unsigned legs.

Seeing it: \(x(t) = t^3 - 6t^2 + 9t\)

Turnarounds are the local max/min of \(x(t)\) — exactly where \(v = x'(t) = 0\). The journey: \(0 \to 4 \to 0 \to 4\).

Checkpoint 1 — your turn

Try it! (≈5 min)

A particle moves with \(x(t) = t^2 - 4t + 3\) metres, \(t \geq 0\) seconds.

  • (a) When is the particle at rest, and where is it then?
  • (b) Find the displacement and the distance travelled over the first \(4\) seconds.
  • (a) \(v = 2t - 4 = 0 \Rightarrow t = 2\) s, at \(x(2) = -1\) m (one metre left of \(O\)).
  • (b) \(x(0) = 3\), \(x(2) = -1\), \(x(4) = 3\): displacement \(= 3 - 3 = 0\); distance \(= 4 + 4 = 8\) m.
  • Moral: zero displacement never means the particle stayed home.

Lesson 2 · 12B–12C

Constant acceleration and \(v\)–\(t\) graphs

  • The five constant-acceleration formulas — and where they come from.
  • \(v\)–\(t\) graphs: gradient is acceleration, area is displacement.
  • Signed area vs total area = displacement vs distance.

Constant acceleration: the five formulas

Only when \(a\) is constant:

\[v = u + at \qquad s = ut + \tfrac{1}{2}at^2\]

\[v^2 = u^2 + 2as \qquad s = \tfrac{1}{2}(u+v)t\]

Try it!

Derive them from \(a = \dfrac{dv}{dt}\) by antidifferentiating. Each one is a calculus fact, not a physics fact.

Example 2

A car travelling at \(20\) m/s brakes with constant deceleration \(4\) m/s². Time and distance to stop?

  • Stop means \(v = 0\): \(0 = 20 - 4t \Rightarrow t = 5\) s.
  • No time needed for distance: \(0 = 20^2 - 2(4)s \Rightarrow s = 50\) m.
  • Formula choice = which variable is absent: \(v^2 = u^2 + 2as\) skips \(t\).
Lesson 2 · Constant accelerationVCE Specialist Mathematics 3&4

Velocity–time graphs

  • Gradient of the \(v\)–\(t\) graph = acceleration.
  • Signed area between graph and \(t\)-axis = displacement: \(\int_{t_1}^{t_2} v\,dt\).
  • Total area (all pieces counted positive) = distance travelled: \(\int_{t_1}^{t_2} |v|\,dt\).
Example — next slide

\(v(t) = 3(t-1)(t-3)\) on \([0,4]\) — the velocity of Example 1 — drawn with its signed areas \(+4, -4, +4\): displacement \(4\) m, distance \(12\) m.

Exam habit

Sketch \(v(t)\) first. The sign changes of \(v\) organise the whole question.

Displacement \(= +4 - 4 + 4 = 4\) m (signed)  ·  distance \(= 4 + 4 + 4 = 12\) m (all areas positive)

Checkpoint 2 — your turn

Try it! (≈5 min)
  • (a) A train accelerates from rest at \(2.5\) m/s² over \(200\) m. Find its final speed and the time taken.
  • (b) A car doing \(30\) m/s brakes uniformly to a stop in \(90\) m. Find the deceleration and the stopping time.
  • (a) \(v^2 = 0 + 2(2.5)(200) = 1000 \Rightarrow v = 10\sqrt{10} \approx 31.6\) m/s; then \(t = \dfrac{v}{a} = \dfrac{10\sqrt{10}}{2.5} \approx 12.6\) s.
  • (b) \(0 = 30^2 + 2a(90) \Rightarrow a = -5\) m/s²; \(0 = 30 - 5t \Rightarrow t = 6\) s.
  • Sanity check via areas: stopping distance \(= \tfrac12 (30)(6) = 90\) m ✓ — the triangle under the \(v\)–\(t\) graph.

Lesson 3 · 12D–12E

Kinematics meets differential equations

  • Three expressions for acceleration — and how to pick the right one.
  • Solve \(a = f(v)\) and \(a = f(x)\) by separation.
  • Meet simple harmonic motion in disguise.

Three expressions for acceleration

\[a = \frac{dv}{dt} = v\frac{dv}{dx} = \frac{d}{dx}\!\left(\tfrac{1}{2}v^2\right)\]

  • Chain rule does the work: \(\dfrac{dv}{dt} = \dfrac{dv}{dx}\cdot\dfrac{dx}{dt} = v\dfrac{dv}{dx}\).
  • And \(\dfrac{d}{dx}\left(\tfrac12 v^2\right) = v\dfrac{dv}{dx}\) by the chain rule again.
Which form to choose

Given \(a\) as a function of \(t\) → use \(\dfrac{dv}{dt}\).   Given \(a = f(v)\) → use \(\dfrac{dv}{dt}\) (want \(v(t)\)) or \(v\dfrac{dv}{dx}\) (want \(v(x)\)).   Given \(a = f(x)\) → use \(\dfrac{d}{dx}\left(\tfrac12 v^2\right)\).

Lesson 3 · Kinematics meets DEsVCE Specialist Mathematics 3&4

Worked example: \(a = f(v)\)

Example 3

A body decelerates so that \(a = -0.1v^2\). At \(x = 0\) its velocity is \(20\) m/s. Find \(v\) as a function of \(x\), then \(v\) as a function of \(t\).

  • Position wanted first, so choose \(a = v\dfrac{dv}{dx}\): \(v\dfrac{dv}{dx} = -0.1v^2\); divide by \(v \neq 0\): \(\dfrac{dv}{dx} = -0.1v\).
  • Separate: \(\displaystyle\int \frac{1}{v}\,dv = \int -0.1\,dx \Rightarrow \ln v = -0.1x + c\); \(v(0)=20 \Rightarrow v = 20e^{-0.1x}\).
  • For \(v(t)\), start from \(\dfrac{dv}{dt} = -0.1v^2\): \(\displaystyle\int v^{-2}\,dv = \int -0.1\,dt \Rightarrow -\frac{1}{v} = -0.1t - \frac{1}{20}\).
  • Rearrange: \(v = \dfrac{20}{2t + 1}\). Same motion, two different clocks: one runs on \(x\), one on \(t\).

Worked example: \(a = f(x)\)

Example 4

A particle moves with \(a = -9x\). It starts from rest at \(x = 2\). Find its speed at \(x = 1\).

  • Use \(\dfrac{d}{dx}\left(\tfrac12 v^2\right) = -9x\); antidifferentiate: \(\tfrac12 v^2 = -\tfrac{9}{2}x^2 + c\).
  • Rest at \(x=2\): \(0 = -18 + c \Rightarrow c = 18\), so \(v^2 = 36 - 9x^2\).
  • At \(x = 1\): \(v^2 = 27\), speed \(= 3\sqrt{3}\) m/s.
Notice

\(v^2 = 9(4 - x^2) \geq 0\) forces \(-2 \leq x \leq 2\): the motion is trapped between the turning points. This is simple harmonic motion in disguise.

Lesson 3 · Kinematics meets DEsVCE Specialist Mathematics 3&4

Seeing it: \(v^2 = 9(4 - x^2)\) in the \(x\)–\(v\) plane

The motion lives on an ellipse: turning points at \(x = \pm 2\) (where \(v = 0\)), greatest speed \(6\) m/s at the centre \(x = 0\).

Checkpoint 3 — your turn

Try it! (≈5 min)
  • (a) A body has \(a = -0.2v\) with \(v(0) = 10\) m/s. Find \(v(t)\).
  • (b) A particle has \(a = -4x\) and starts from rest at \(x = 3\). Find \(v^2\) as a function of \(x\), and its speed at the origin.
  • (a) \(\dfrac{dv}{dt} = -0.2v \Rightarrow \ln v = -0.2t + c\), so \(v = 10e^{-0.2t}\) m/s.
  • (b) \(\dfrac{d}{dx}\left(\tfrac12 v^2\right) = -4x \Rightarrow \tfrac12 v^2 = -2x^2 + c\); rest at \(x=3\) gives \(c = 18\), so \(v^2 = 4(9 - x^2)\).
  • At \(x = 0\): \(v^2 = 36\), speed \(= 6\) m/s. Trapped in \(-3 \leq x \leq 3\) — SHM again.

Worked example: from \(a(t)\) all the way to distance

Example 5

A particle has \(a(t) = 2 - 2t\) m/s², with \(v(0) = 3\) and \(x(0) = 0\). Find when it turns around, and the distance travelled in the first 4 seconds.

  • Antidifferentiate: \(v(t) = 3 + 2t - t^2 = -(t-3)(t+1)\); turnaround at \(t = 3\) (reject \(t = -1\)).
  • Again: \(x(t) = 3t + t^2 - \dfrac{t^3}{3}\); evaluate \(x(3) = 9\), \(x(4) = \dfrac{20}{3}\).
  • Distance \(= |x(3) - x(0)| + |x(4) - x(3)| = 9 + \dfrac{7}{3} = \dfrac{34}{3} \approx 11.3\) m.
Chain of custody

Two antidifferentiations, two initial conditions, one turnaround check. Every kinematics chain question has exactly this skeleton.

Seeing it: \(x(t) = 3t + t^2 - \tfrac{t^3}{3}\)

Out to \(x = 9\) by \(t = 3\) (where \(v = 0\)), then back to \(x = \tfrac{20}{3}\): distance \(9 + \tfrac{7}{3} = \tfrac{34}{3}\) m, displacement \(\tfrac{20}{3}\) m.

A particle has \(a(t) = 6t - 6\) m/s², with \(v(0) = 0\) and \(x(0) = 2\), for \(t \geq 0\).

Week 1 summary

  • \(x \xrightarrow{\ d/dt\ } v \xrightarrow{\ d/dt\ } a\), and antidifferentiate to go back (constants fixed by initial conditions).
  • Distance vs displacement: split the journey at every \(v = 0\).
  • Constant-acceleration formulas only when \(a\) is constant — otherwise it's calculus.
  • \(a = \dfrac{dv}{dt} = v\dfrac{dv}{dx} = \dfrac{d}{dx}\left(\tfrac12 v^2\right)\): pick the form that matches what you're given and what you want.
