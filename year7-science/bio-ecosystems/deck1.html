Concept

The seven life processes: MRS GREN

Scientists agree that all living organisms carry out seven life processes. The acronym MRS GREN helps us remember them.

  • Movement — changing position or shape
  • Respiration — releasing energy from food
  • Sensitivity — detecting and responding to the environment
  • Growth — increasing in size or complexity
  • Reproduction — producing offspring
  • Excretion — removing waste products from the body
  • Nutrition — obtaining and using food for energy
Key idea
To be classified as living, an organism must be capable of carrying out all seven life processes at some point in its life cycle.

📚 Booklet p.4 · Q1.3
Work through with the class

Worked example — Is fire alive?

✍️ Use MRS GREN to decide
Fire can grow larger, spread across the ground, and produces gases as it burns. A student says "Fire must be alive!" Use MRS GREN to evaluate this claim.
  • Movement: Fire can spread — but it does not move itself using biological structures. Partially matches — but not biological.
  • Respiration: Fire releases energy from fuel, but not through cellular chemical reactions inside a body. Not biological respiration.
  • Sensitivity: Fire gets bigger when given more fuel, but does not detect and respond using a nervous or sensory system. Does not meet the criterion.
  • Reproduction & Excretion: Fire cannot reproduce itself biologically. It does not have a body that excretes metabolic waste. Fails both criteria.
  • Conclusion: Fire is NOT living. It cannot carry out all seven life processes. It has no cells, cannot reproduce or grow biologically, and has no body to excrete waste.

Your turn

Now you try

✍️ Apply MRS GREN
For each object below, identify whether it is living, dead, or non-living. Use at least two life processes to explain your answer.
  • A sugar glider gliding between trees at night — living, dead, or non-living?
  • Living: it moves (M), respires (R), is sensitive to night/day (S), grows (G), reproduces (R), excretes (E), gets nutrition (N). All seven ✓
  • A dried kangaroo bone — living, dead, or non-living?
  • Dead: was once living (part of a living kangaroo), but can no longer carry out any life processes. Not the same as "non-living."
  • A river stone — living, dead, or non-living?
  • Non-living: has never been alive. Cannot carry out any life processes at all. Not growing, not reproducing, not sensitive.

First Nations perspectives

Country as a living system

Wurundjeri-Woiwurrung people of the Kulin Nation understand Country — the land, water, air, animals, and plants — as a living, interconnected system in which all parts are in relationship.

This is not simply a metaphor. It reflects thousands of years of careful observation of how organisms depend on each other and on non-living conditions — the same insight that underpins modern ecology.

🌎 Connecting to science
When Wurundjeri people say Country is alive, they recognise that living and non-living things interact constantly. This is exactly what biologists study when they investigate ecosystems. Both perspectives agree that removing or disturbing one part affects the whole.
💬 Reflect
What is one similarity and one difference between the Wurundjeri understanding of "living Country" and the scientific idea of a "living organism"?

Concept

Dead vs non-living — an important distinction

Common mistake
A dried leaf is dead, not non-living — it was once part of a living tree. A rock has never been alive — it is non-living. These are not the same thing!
📚 Booklet p.4 · Year 8 extension
Year 8 Extension

Cells — the basic unit of life

All living organisms are made of one or more cells. The cell is the smallest unit that can carry out all the processes of life.

  • Unicellular organisms: made of a single cell (e.g. bacteria, amoeba)
  • Multicellular organisms: made of many cells working together (e.g. plants, animals, fungi)
  • Cells are surrounded by a cell membrane that controls what enters and leaves
  • Most cells contain DNA — the instructions for life
Cell theory (Year 8)
1. All living things are made of cells.
2. The cell is the basic unit of life.
3. All cells come from pre-existing cells.
💬 Extension challenge
If a cell is the smallest unit of life, does that mean a single cell removed from a wombat is "alive"? What would it need to stay alive?
📚 Booklet p.4 · Q3
Year 8 Extension

Plant cells vs animal cells

Both animal cells and plant cells carry out all seven MRS GREN processes. But plant cells have extra structures that animal cells don't — some of these are exactly what allow plants to photosynthesise.

Answering Q3 — two structural differences that allow photosynthesis
Chloroplasts — contain chlorophyll and are the site of photosynthesis, capturing sunlight to make food. Cell wall — a rigid cellulose layer that supports the plant and holds leaves upright, positioning the chloroplasts to capture sunlight.
💬 Extension challenge
Plant cells have mitochondria AND chloroplasts, but animal cells only have mitochondria. Using the Nutrition and Respiration processes from MRS GREN, explain why plant cells need both.

Year 8 Extension

The virus debate — living or not?

Arguments for "living"

  • Contain DNA or RNA (genetic material)
  • Can evolve and mutate over time
  • Can reproduce — but only inside a host cell

Arguments for "non-living"

  • Have no cells — violates cell theory
  • Cannot carry out ANY life processes independently
  • No metabolism — cannot respire, eat, grow or excrete on their own
Scientific consensus
Most scientists classify viruses as non-living, or in a category of their own. They are biological entities — not quite alive, not quite non-living. The debate is ongoing!
💬 Discuss
Does the MRS GREN framework help classify viruses? Which steps does it fail at? Does this tell us MRS GREN has a limitation?
