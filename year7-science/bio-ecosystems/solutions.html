Q1Explain a difficult-to-classify item using MRS GREN3 marks

Answers vary — award for a reasoned justification. Model (virus): A virus can reproduce (R) — but only by hijacking a host cell — and it contains genetic material. However it shows no independent movement, respiration, growth, excretion, sensitivity or nutrition, and has no cells. Because it cannot carry out all seven processes on its own, it is best classified as non-living.

1 mark: criteria met · 1 mark: criteria not met · 1 mark: justified final classification.

Q2Is a virus living or non-living? Explain using MRS GREN2 marks

Non-living. A virus can only reproduce inside a host cell and cannot independently move, grow, respire, excrete, sense, or obtain nutrition. It has no cells, so it fails most MRS GREN criteria and cannot function as an organism on its own.

Q3Yr 8Two structural differences that let plant cells photosynthesise3 marks

Chloroplasts — contain the green pigment chlorophyll and are the site of photosynthesis, capturing light energy to make glucose. Animal cells have none.

Cell wall (rigid cellulose) — supports the plant and holds leaves upright and spread out so chloroplasts are positioned to capture sunlight. (Accept large permanent vacuole, which keeps cells turgid to hold leaves up to the light.)

1 mark per structure named + 1 mark for linking a structure to photosynthesis.

Q4Yr 8Evaluate: should viruses be a "seventh kingdom"?3 marks

For: viruses have genetic material (DNA/RNA), evolve and mutate, and interact with living organisms — features shared with life.

Against: they have no cellular structure (violating cell theory), no metabolism, and cannot reproduce or do anything without hijacking a host cell.

Evaluation: most scientists reject a seventh kingdom — kingdoms are cellular, and viruses are non-cellular. A defensible conclusion either way earns marks if supported with this evidence.

Q1At which level does the echidna first differ from the kangaroo?4 marks

They share Kingdom Animalia, Phylum Chordata and Class Mammalia — both are backboned mammals. They first differ at Order: the kangaroo is Diprotodontia, the echidna is Monotremata.

Because they diverge at Order (a fairly broad level), they share a more distant common ancestor than two animals in the same family or genus would. They are related — both mammals — but not closely.

Q2Why classify into a hierarchy rather than give each organism a unique name?2 marks

A hierarchy groups related organisms together, showing evolutionary relationships and letting us predict shared characteristics. With millions of species, nested groups make information far easier to organise, search and communicate than millions of unrelated names.

Q3First NationsThe three domains — classify human, E. coli, and a thermophile3 marks

Domains are separated by cell type / basic cell structure and chemistry — principally whether cells have a membrane-bound nucleus, plus differences in cell wall and membrane chemistry.

  • Human → Eukarya (cells have a nucleus)
  • E. coli → Bacteria (prokaryote)
  • Hot-spring thermophile (Sulfolobus) → Archaea (prokaryote adapted to extreme conditions)
Q4Yr 8A DNA reclassification example, and why DNA beats appearance3 marks

Example (accept any valid one): whales were shown by DNA to be close relatives of hippopotamuses rather than of fish; or fungi were moved out of the plant kingdom; or several bird groups were reshuffled after DNA sequencing.

DNA is more reliable than physical appearance because unrelated species can look alike through convergent evolution (similar environments produce similar body shapes), whereas DNA sequences directly record shared inheritance from a common ancestor.

Q2Why must scientists worldwide use the same naming system?2 marks

A single international system means every scientist refers to exactly the same species, regardless of language. Example: "magpie" means an unrelated bird in Australia versus Europe, so common names cause confusion — but Cracticus tibicen is unambiguous everywhere.

Q3Two features that make fungi more different from plants than they appear3 marks

1. Nutrition: fungi cannot photosynthesise — they are heterotrophs that digest organic matter externally and absorb the nutrients, whereas plants make their own food. 2. Cell walls: fungal cell walls are made of chitin, not cellulose like plants. (Also: fungi store glycogen, plants store starch.)

These fundamental differences in how they feed and what they are built from justify placing fungi in a separate kingdom.

Q4Yr 8Diagram: one key difference between prokaryotic and eukaryotic cells3 marks

Key difference: the eukaryotic cell has a membrane-bound nucleus enclosing its DNA; the prokaryotic cell has no nucleus — its DNA floats free in the cytoplasm (a nucleoid).

A correct labelled sketch shows: prokaryote (cell wall, membrane, free DNA loop, no nucleus) vs eukaryote (membrane, nucleus containing DNA). This nucleus/no-nucleus split is the basis of the domain system because it reflects the deepest division in the history of life.

1 mark diagram · 1 mark correct labels · 1 mark explanation.

Q2One advantage and one disadvantage of the flowchart format2 marks

Advantage: the branching layout is visual and easy to follow — you can see the whole path at a glance. Disadvantage: it takes up much more space and becomes cluttered and hard to draw when there are many species.

Q3How within-species variation limits a key, and how to improve it4 marks

If a key relies on a feature that varies within a species — e.g. juvenile vs adult colouring, or males vs females (sexual dimorphism, as in the superb fairy-wren where only breeding males are bright blue) — a user could be sent down the wrong branch and misidentify the organism.

Improvement: base questions on stable structural features (beak shape, body size, number of legs) that don't change with age or sex, or add separate branches for each life stage/sex.

Q4Compare a phylogenetic tree with a dichotomous key3 marks

A dichotomous key tells you which species an unknown organism is, using observable features — but says nothing about how species are related. A phylogenetic tree shows evolutionary relationships and common ancestry from DNA — but you can't use it to identify an unknown specimen in the field. Each answers a different question.

Q5Yr 8Using DNA barcoding to identify an unknown beetle3 marks

Extract DNA from the beetle, sequence a short standard region (e.g. the COI gene), and compare it against a reference database; the closest match identifies the species.

Advantage: works from any life stage or a damaged fragment and is highly precise. Limitation: needs a lab, equipment and an existing reference database — if the species has never been barcoded, there is no match (a field key needs none of this).

Q1Why is a rotting log biotic even though it is not alive?2 marks

Because it is made of organic material from a once-living organism (a tree). Biotic factors include living things and the dead organic matter and products that come from them, which still supply nutrients and habitat.

Q3Why isn't a 50-species forest necessarily more biodiverse than a 30-species one?2 marks

Species richness is only one part of biodiversity. You also need to know evenness (relative abundance) — a forest where one species dominates is less diverse than an even one — as well as genetic and ecosystem diversity.

Q4Yr 8Calculate the Simpson Diversity Index for two quadrats3 marks
Quadrat A (N = 20; species 10, 5, 3, 2)
Σ(n/N)² = 0.5² + 0.25² + 0.15² + 0.1² = 0.25 + 0.0625 + 0.0225 + 0.01 = 0.345
D = 1 − 0.345 = 0.655

Quadrat B (N = 20; species 18, 2)
Σ(n/N)² = 0.9² + 0.1² = 0.81 + 0.01 = 0.82
D = 1 − 0.82 = 0.18

A higher D means greater diversity. Quadrat A (0.66) is far more diverse than Quadrat B (0.18): A has more species and they are more evenly spread, while B is dominated by one species.

Q1Interpret the woodland food web (Figure 6.1)4 marks

(a) If grasshoppers fell by 80%, the brown falcon population would likely decline because it loses a major food source — unless it can switch to alternative prey. Less food means lower survival and breeding success.

(b) An apex predator sits at the top of the web with no natural predators. Losing it is more significant than losing a primary consumer because it controls the numbers of species below it — its removal triggers a trophic cascade that ripples through many levels.

(c) An omnivore such as the lace monitor can be both: when it eats wattle seeds/plant matter it is a primary consumer, and when it eats grasshoppers or small animals it is a secondary consumer.

Q2Why do scientists prefer food webs over food chains?2 marks

A food web shows the many interconnected feeding relationships in a real ecosystem, including alternative food sources. This makes it more realistic and lets scientists predict how a change to one species ripples out to others — a single chain shows only one pathway.

Q3The 10% rule — energy calculations4 marks
Producers: 10,000 units
(a) Primary consumer level = 10% × 10,000 = 1,000 units
Secondary consumer level = 10% × 1,000 = 100 units
(b) Tertiary consumer level = 10% × 100 = 10 units

About 90% of energy is lost at each transfer (as heat, movement and undigested waste), so only a tiny fraction reaches the top.

Q4Yr 8Define "keystone species" with an Australian example3 marks

A keystone species has an effect on its ecosystem far larger than its numbers would suggest; removing it causes the ecosystem to change dramatically.

Example: the dingo. As a top predator it suppresses foxes and cats and controls kangaroo numbers. Remove dingoes and fox/cat numbers rise, small native mammals are hunted out and overgrazing increases — a trophic cascade that reshapes the whole community. (Accept digging mammals such as bilbies/bettongs as ecosystem engineers.)

Q2Explain the link between biodiversity and ecosystem services3 marks

Ecosystem services are produced by living organisms, so higher biodiversity generally means services are delivered more reliably. Diversity provides redundancy — if one species is lost, others can fill its role — making the ecosystem more resilient. As biodiversity declines, this backup is lost, roles go unfilled, and services (pollination, water purification, nutrient cycling) break down.

Q3Yr 8Evaluate assigning dollar values to ecosystem services3 marks

Advantages: (1) it makes nature's value visible in economic and political decisions so it isn't treated as free; (2) it allows the cost of destroying an ecosystem to be compared with the cost of protecting it.

Limitation: many values — cultural, spiritual, and the intrinsic worth of species — cannot truly be captured in dollars, and pricing nature risks treating it as a commodity to be traded away.

Q4Yr 8A Victorian ecosystem near a tipping point, and how to prevent the shift3 marks

Example (accept any valid one): mountain ash forests — repeated intense bushfires can shift them permanently to lower Acacia scrub because the ash cannot regenerate if burnt before it sets seed. (Or the Gippsland Lakes tipping into recurrent algal-bloom "dead zones".)

Management: stop logging old-growth, reduce ignition risk and fuel loads, and protect unburnt refuges — keeping the system below the threshold where change becomes self-reinforcing.

Q2Two mountain pygmy possum adaptations, useless in a Melbourne garden3 marks

1. Hibernation under snow — in the alps it survives freezing winters beneath an insulating snow layer; in a warm suburb there is no snow and no need to hibernate, so it would waste energy or fail to trigger correctly. 2. Feeding on Bogong moths / fat storage — it relies on the seasonal moth migration to the alps; in a suburb those moths aren't reliably available, so this specialised feeding strategy fails. (Also thick fur would cause overheating.)

Q3Explain natural selection using the ringtail possum colour scenario4 marks

Variation: possums vary — some darker, some paler. Heritability: colour is inherited and passed to offspring. Selection pressure: the new eagle predator spots pale possums more easily against dark granite, so they are eaten more often. Differential reproduction: darker (better-camouflaged) possums survive and breed more, passing on dark colouring. Over 50 generations the population becomes predominantly darker.

Q4Yr 8Marsupial mole vs European mole — convergent evolution3 marks

Although only distantly related, both moles face the same selection pressures of a burrowing, underground life, so natural selection independently produced very similar features (cylindrical body, powerful digging limbs, reduced eyes). This is convergent evolution — it shows that similar environments shape unrelated species toward similar forms, and that looking alike does not prove close relationship.

Q1Trace the ecological effects of the red fox (impact chain)6 marks

Direct effect on native prey: foxes prey heavily on small-to-medium mammals (bandicoots, bettongs, potoroos) and ground-nesting birds that evolved no defences against a placental predator — causing steep declines and local extinctions.

Effect on plant communities: many of those small mammals dig, disperse seeds and spread fungal spores; as they disappear, seed dispersal and soil turnover fall and vegetation structure changes.

Effect on other animals: foxes compete with and suppress native predators such as quolls, and their presence reshapes the whole community (a mesopredator/prey effect rippling through the web).

Q2Why are native mammals so vulnerable to foxes, when rabbits survive?3 marks

Native Australian mammals evolved without placental predators like the fox, so they lack anti-predator behaviours and many breed slowly — they cannot replace losses fast enough. European rabbits co-evolved alongside foxes in Europe, so they are naturally wary and breed extremely rapidly, letting their populations absorb heavy predation.

Q3Yr 8Evaluate the myxoma virus as biological control of rabbits4 marks

How it works: the myxoma virus causes myxomatosis, a fatal disease spread between rabbits by mosquitoes and fleas.

Evidence of effectiveness: after release in the early 1950s it killed an estimated 99% of infected rabbits and crashed Australia's rabbit population.

Risks: rabbits then evolved resistance and the virus became less lethal, so effectiveness fell; biological controls can also potentially harm non-target species or mutate, so they require careful host-specificity testing before release.

Q4Yr 8Why are island species so vulnerable to introduced predators?3 marks

Island populations are usually small (little genetic diversity, so limited ability to adapt), and they evolved in isolation without such predators, so they have no defensive behaviours. If they are wiped out they cannot be re-colonised from elsewhere because they are cut off. Victorian example: little penguins on Phillip Island were devastated by foxes crossing to the colony until intensive fox control was introduced.

Q1Why does fragmented Reserve B support fewer species than continuous Reserve A?3 marks

Even though the total area is the same, four small 10 ha blocks suffer far more edge effect (altered wind, light and moisture favouring weeds and predators), so less true interior habitat remains. Small patches cannot support viable populations of species that need large territories, and the farmland between blocks isolates populations, preventing movement and gene flow and leaving them more vulnerable to local extinction.

Q2Trace the eutrophication chain in the Gippsland Lakes4 marks

(a) Nutrient input: nitrogen and phosphorus enter from fertiliser runoff and sewage. (b) Effect on algae: the extra nutrients trigger a rapid algal bloom. (c) Water clarity and plants: thick algae block light, so submerged aquatic plants can't photosynthesise and die. (d) Oxygen and animals: when the algae die, decomposing bacteria consume the dissolved oxygen, creating a low-oxygen "dead zone" where fish and other aquatic animals suffocate (fish kills).

Q3A Victorian species facing multiple threats — how they interact4 marks

Model — Leadbeater's possum: it needs hollows that form only in mountain ash over 150 years old. Threat 1 (logging) removes old hollow-bearing trees; Threat 2 (bushfire) — the 2009 fires burned ~45% of its habitat and hollows take 150+ years to reform; Threat 3 (climate change) increases fire frequency so regrowth is burnt before it is old enough to form hollows.

Why the combination is worse: each threat alone is survivable, but together they trap the species — the forest is repeatedly reset before it can produce the old trees the possum depends on, accelerating decline toward extinction. (Accept any Victorian species with two well-explained interacting threats.)

Q4Yr 8Three risks facing a small, isolated bandicoot population4 marks
  • Loss of genetic diversity: random genetic drift in a small population reduces variation, limiting the ability to adapt.
  • Inbreeding depression: mating between close relatives exposes harmful recessive alleles, lowering health and fertility.
  • Demographic stochasticity: chance events (a run of one-sex births, a bad season) can crash a tiny population.
  • Allee effect: at very low density individuals struggle to find mates, so the population grows even more slowly — a downward spiral.
Q5Yr 8Planetary boundaries: biodiversity as the most-exceeded boundary3 marks

It means humans have already pushed biodiversity loss well beyond the safe operating limit for a stable planet. Crossing it makes other boundaries harder to stay within because biodiversity underpins the processes that regulate them — forests and ocean life store carbon (climate) and healthy ecosystems cycle and purify water (freshwater). As biodiversity collapses, those regulating functions weaken, worsening climate change and freshwater stress in turn.

11Conservation & Action
Task 11.1Entry taskRank the conservation strategies and justify6 marks

No single correct ranking — award marks for a coherent, justified order. A strong response tends to rank in-situ protection (new national park; habitat corridors) highly because it protects whole ecosystems and natural processes, treats legislation banning key pesticides as powerful but dependent on enforcement, and places ex-situ measures (captive breeding, seed/gene banks) and education as valuable supports rather than complete solutions on their own.

Justify top choice: the best answers argue that protecting habitat in place keeps species in their natural web of relationships, whereas ex-situ methods only preserve a species until its habitat is restored.

Q1The eastern barred bandicoot recovery program6 marks

(a) Both. Captive breeding at Zoos Victoria is ex-situ (away from the wild), while reintroduction into predator-free enclosures is in-situ (back into natural habitat).

(b) The ultimate goal is a self-sustaining wild population. Before reintroduction can succeed, the original threats must be controlled (fox and cat control), there must be suitable protected habitat, and released animals need enough genetic diversity to remain healthy.

(c) Reaching over 1,500 by 2022 shows the strategy worked — the species was downlisted from extinct-in-the-wild. Remaining concerns: the population still depends on fenced/predator-free sites and ongoing management, and it may carry reduced genetic diversity from its founder bottleneck.

Q2Design a realistic local conservation action (northern Melbourne)4 marks

Award for a realistic, well-reasoned plan. Model:

(a) Action: plant an indigenous-vegetation corridor of local grasses, wattles and eucalypts along the school grounds or a nearby creek, and install nest boxes.

(b) Benefit (linked to ecology): it creates habitat and food, and links isolated patches so native animals can move safely between them — restoring gene flow and supporting the local food web (Sections 5, 6 and 10).

(c) Limitation: corridors need ongoing weed and watering maintenance, can also help weeds or predators spread, and take years to become effective habitat.

Q3Yr 8Evaluate the EPBC Act 19994 marks

What it does: it is Australia's main federal environmental law, protecting nationally threatened species and ecological communities and requiring assessment of developments likely to harm them.

Evidence it is failing: biodiversity has kept declining and many listed species continue toward extinction; the 2020 independent (Samuel) review found it "not fit for purpose", citing weak enforcement and slow, under-resourced processes.

One improvement: establish an independent regulator with legally binding national environmental standards (accept: more funding, faster listing, stronger penalties).

Q4Yr 8Evaluate rewilding Tasmanian devils to mainland Victoria4 marks

(a) Proposed benefit: as a mesopredator/scavenger the devil could suppress foxes and feral cats, triggering a trophic cascade that helps small native mammals recover.

(b) Community concerns: possible predation on livestock and pets, uncertainty about effects on native prey, and disease risk (to and from the devils).

(c) Decision-making authority: it should be shared — ecologists and government for the science and regulation, and especially Traditional Owners and local communities, because they hold responsibility for Country and would live with the consequences. A defensible view earns marks if justified.

(a) Producer: wallaby grass. Apex predator: wedge-tailed eagle. (1)

(b) If brown falcons were removed, grasshopper numbers would rise — a predator that ate them is gone, so more survive and breed. (2)

B·Q3European carp impact chain (≥3 steps)4 marks

Step 1: carp stir up river sediment as they feed, increasing turbidity (cloudiness) and releasing stored nutrients. Step 2: less light reaches the riverbed, so aquatic plants can't photosynthesise and die, removing food and shelter. Step 3: native fish and invertebrates lose habitat, and released nutrients fuel algal blooms whose decay lowers oxygen — causing fish kills. Native species decline.

B·Q4Structural vs behavioural adaptation, with examples4 marks

A structural adaptation is an inherited physical body feature; a behavioural adaptation is an inherited pattern of action. Structural example: the echidna's sharp spines protect it from predators. Behavioural example: the echidna entering torpor in winter (or a kangaroo resting in shade during the day) to conserve energy/water.

B·Q5First NationsThree ways Caring for Country supports biodiversity3 marks
  • Cultural burning creates a mosaic of habitats at different regrowth stages, supporting complex, resilient food webs.
  • Seasonal, sustainable harvesting (strict protocols against over-harvesting) keeps populations healthy.
  • Protecting sacred sites and totemic species and active, ongoing land management maintain habitat and species over the long term.
B·Q6Three categories of ecosystem service, with Victorian examples3 marks

Any three of: Provisioning — Melbourne's drinking water from mountain ash catchments; Regulating — mountain ash storing carbon / wetlands controlling floods; Cultural — Great Ocean Road tourism / the Birrarung's cultural significance; Supporting — decomposers recycling nutrients into Victorian soils.

C·Q7Declining wetland ecosystem (extended response)6 marks

(a) Two ecosystem services (2): water purification/filtering (regulating) and flood control (regulating); accept habitat/biodiversity support, fish provisioning, or cultural value.

(b) Food-web changes from carp (3): carp stir sediment → higher turbidity → aquatic plants die (loss of food and shelter); native fish and waterbirds decline; nutrient release & algal blooms lower oxygen, causing fish kills. (Two specific effects required.)

(c) One restoration strategy (1): remove/reduce carp (e.g. screening or targeted control) and replant native aquatic vegetation — restoring water clarity, habitat and the native food web.

C·Q8Leadbeater's possum (extended response)6 marks

(a) Two threats, each explained (4): logging of old-growth mountain ash removes the hollow-bearing trees it nests in (hollows take 150+ years to form); bushfire (e.g. 2009) destroys large areas of habitat and kills old trees faster than they can be replaced. (Accept climate change increasing fire frequency.)

(b) Effect on species diversity (2): losing the possum lowers species richness directly, and because it is part of the food web (eating invertebrates, prey for owls) and an indicator of old-growth health, its loss signals decline that also threatens other hollow-dependent species.

(a) One integrated example (2): cultural burning combined with modern fire management to sustain habitat mosaics and food-web complexity; or Indigenous Protected Areas, which research shows hold higher biodiversity than areas managed by agencies alone; or Wurundjeri Elders guiding ecological restoration projects.

(b) One challenge (2): the two systems use different frameworks and evidence (oral, relational knowledge vs written data), and integration can become tokenistic without genuine authority-sharing and self-determination for First Nations peoples.

(c) Overall judgement (2): award for a clear position supported by the unit's evidence — strong answers agree the statement is justified, citing the higher biodiversity of Country managed by First Nations peoples, while noting integration must be done respectfully and on equal terms.
