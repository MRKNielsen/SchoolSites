📚 Booklet p.25 · §8.1
Concept

Three types of adaptations

Key idea
An adaptation must be inherited (passed from parent to offspring) and must increase the organism's chances of surviving and reproducing in its specific environment. Something that simply varies between individuals is not an adaptation unless natural selection has acted on it over many generations.
📚 Booklet p.26 · §8.2
Victorian case study

Mountain pygmy possum — adapted to the alpine zone

The mountain pygmy possum (Burramys parvus) lives only in rocky alpine habitats above 1,400 m — Mount Bogong, Mount Higginbotham, Mount Buller. It is the only Australian mammal found exclusively in alpine regions.

  • Structural: Dense fur traps heat in sub-zero temperatures
  • Structural: Specialised tongue extracts seeds from Bogong moth exoskeletons
  • Behavioural: Migrates vertically — moves to low rocky outcrops in winter
  • Physiological: Hibernates for up to 7 months — lowers heart rate to 2 beats per minute and body temperature to near 2°C
Climate change threat
Snow cover is declining in the Victorian alps. The mountain pygmy possum needs snow as an insulating blanket during hibernation — without snow, they freeze. They also depend on Bogong moths as a pre-hibernation food source. Bogong moth migration has become erratic due to drought. A species perfectly adapted to one narrow environment is extremely vulnerable to that environment changing.
💬 Analyse
Which of the mountain pygmy possum's adaptations would be MOST threatened by declining snow cover? Explain your reasoning.
📚 Booklet p.27 · Q8.3
Work through with the class

Worked example — classifying echidna adaptations

✍️ Classify and explain each adaptation
The short-beaked echidna (Tachyglossus aculeatus) lives across Victoria in a wide range of habitats. Classify each adaptation and explain its survival benefit.
  • Spines: Structural — modified hairs made of keratin. Deter predators; echidna rolls into a ball so only spines are exposed. Survival benefit: defence against eagles, quolls, and foxes.
  • Long sticky tongue (24 cm): Structural — reaches deep into ant and termite mounds. Survival benefit: accesses a food source unavailable to competitors with shorter tongues.
  • Torpor in winter: Behavioural AND physiological — the echidna chooses to shelter and reduces metabolic rate. Survival benefit: conserves energy when ants are less active and cold temperatures make hunting costly.
  • Key takeaway: Many organisms have adaptations in ALL three categories working together. The echidna's spines (structural), torpor behaviour (behavioural), and reduced metabolism (physiological) all contribute to survival in different ways.

Your turn

Now you try — platypus adaptations

✍️ Classify and explain platypus adaptations
The platypus (Ornithorhynchus anatinus) lives in Victorian rivers including the Yarra, Maribyrnong, and Mitchell rivers. For each adaptation below, classify it and explain its survival benefit.
  • Bill with electroreceptors: Structural — detects tiny electrical fields produced by muscle contractions of prey (shrimps, insect larvae) in murky water. Enables hunting without sight.
  • Webbed feet: Structural — webbing folds back on land for walking, extends for swimming. Dual function for both aquatic and terrestrial movement.
  • Dense waterproof fur: Structural — two layers; outer repels water, inner insulates in cold Victorian waterways. Maintains body temperature while diving.
  • Nocturnal activity: Behavioural — reduces encounters with diurnal (daytime) predators such as hawks, and allows use of electroreception in low-light conditions.

First Nations perspectives

Deep ecological knowledge — reading animal adaptations

Wurundjeri-Woiwurrung people accumulated thousands of years of detailed knowledge about the adaptations and behaviours of animals across Country. This knowledge was essential for hunting, harvesting, and understanding seasonal change.

For example, the movement and behaviour of Bogong moths (Agrotis infusa) was a reliable seasonal indicator. Bogong moths migrate to alpine regions each spring, and Wurundjeri and other Kulin Nation peoples would travel to the alps for moth festivals — harvesting moths that had gathered in rock crevices as a rich, high-fat food source.

🌎 Adaptations as ecological calendar

Knowing when and why species behave the way they do — their adaptations and seasonal patterns — allowed Wurundjeri-Woiwurrung people to manage harvesting sustainably. They understood that:

  • Bogong moths aggregate in alps due to physiological adaptations to heat
  • Platypus behaviour varies by season and water temperature
  • Echidnas' torpor timing signals temperature thresholds

This is the same knowledge that ecologists now study — built over generations of careful observation.

📚 Booklet p.27 · Year 8 extension
Year 8 Extension

How adaptations arise — natural selection

Adaptations are not chosen or learned by individuals. They arise through natural selection over many generations:

  1. Variation: Individuals in a population differ (e.g. some wombats have slightly thicker fur)
  2. Heritability: Some of that variation is inherited from parents
  3. Selection: Individuals with traits better suited to the environment survive and reproduce more
  4. Differential reproduction: The useful trait becomes more common in subsequent generations

Over thousands of generations, this produces highly specialised adaptations — like the platypus bill or the mountain pygmy possum's extreme cold-tolerance.

Key distinction
Natural selection works on inherited variation. A wombat that grows thicker fur because it lived in a cold environment does NOT pass on that fur to offspring (this would be Lamarckism — an incorrect idea). Only if there was genetic variation for fur thickness, and thicker fur increased survival, would natural selection act over generations.
📚 Booklet p.27 · Year 8 extension
Year 8 Extension

Convergent evolution — same solution, different origins

Convergent evolution occurs when unrelated species independently evolve similar adaptations in response to similar environmental pressures.

  • Dolphin fin + shark fin + ichthyosaur fin — all streamlined for fast swimming, but dolphins are mammals, sharks are fish, ichthyosaurs were reptiles
  • Eyes have evolved independently in vertebrates, cephalopods (octopus), and insects
  • Australian sugar glider vs. North American flying squirrel — both evolved gliding membranes independently
Australian example
Marsupial moles (Notoryctes typhlops) and placental moles (Talpidae, Europe/Asia) look nearly identical — small, cylindrical, tiny eyes, shovel-like forelimbs. They evolved from entirely different ancestors in response to the same selection pressure: burrowing in sandy soil.
💬 Challenge
Convergent evolution is evidence for natural selection. Explain why: if two unrelated species independently arrived at the same body shape, what does that tell us about the environment?
Section 8 · Recap

What we covered

  • An adaptation is an inherited feature (physical, behavioural, or physiological) that increases an organism's survival and reproduction in its specific environment
  • Structural = body features; Behavioural = actions; Physiological = internal processes — most organisms have all three types
  • Victorian examples: mountain pygmy possum (hibernation, dense fur), platypus (electroreceptor bill, webbed feet), echidna (spines, torpor)
  • Wurundjeri-Woiwurrung knowledge of animal adaptations and behaviour underpinned sustainable harvesting of resources like Bogong moths
