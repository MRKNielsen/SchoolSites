📚 Booklet p.5 · §2.1
Concept

Why do scientists classify living things?

Scientists have discovered over 8 million species on Earth — with more being found every year. To make sense of this diversity, we need a system.

The Linnaean classification system, developed by Carl Linnaeus in the 18th century, groups organisms by shared features.

  • Understand how organisms are related
  • Compare different types of organisms
  • Communicate clearly across languages
  • Identify newly discovered species
Key person
Carl Linnaeus (1707–1778) was a Swedish botanist who developed the classification system still in use today. He gave every known species a two-part Latin name.
💬 Think
Why might using a language like Latin (that no one speaks as a native tongue) be useful for scientific names?

📚 Booklet p.7 · Q2.2
Work through with the class

Worked example — classifying the kangaroo

✍️ Classify the eastern grey kangaroo step by step
Place the eastern grey kangaroo (Macropus giganteus) into each level of the Linnaean system and give a reason for each.
  • Kingdom Animalia — it is multicellular, eats other organisms for energy, and moves. It is an animal.
  • Phylum Chordata — it has a backbone (spinal cord). So do birds, fish, reptiles, and humans.
  • Class Mammalia — it is warm-blooded, has fur, and suckles its young with milk.
  • Order Diprotodontia — it has two large incisors at the front. Wombats and possums are also in this order.
  • Family Macropodidae / Genus Macropus / Species giganteus — Macropus giganteus, the eastern grey kangaroo. "Macropus" means large-footed; "giganteus" means giant.

📚 Booklet p.7 · Q2.3
Your turn

Now you try — the echidna

✍️ Classify the short-beaked echidna
The echidna (Tachyglossus aculeatus) has fur, is warm-blooded, lays eggs but suckles its young, and has a backbone. Fill in as many levels as you can.
  • Kingdom: Animalia — it eats, moves, and is multicellular.
  • Phylum: Chordata — it has a backbone.
  • Class: Mammalia — it has fur and suckles young, even though it lays eggs (it is a monotreme).
  • Order Monotremata — egg-laying mammals. Only three species exist: echidna, long-beaked echidna, and platypus. Highly unique to Australia!

First Nations perspectives

Indigenous knowledge and classification

Long before Linnaeus, Wurundjeri-Woiwurrung people had detailed systems for naming, grouping, and understanding the organisms on Country.

These knowledge systems were built over thousands of generations of careful observation. They include:

  • Names for hundreds of plants, animals, and fungi on Kulin Country
  • Understanding of seasonal behaviours and relationships between species
  • Knowledge of which plants flower together, which animals move together
🌎 Connecting to Linnaean classification
Both Indigenous and Linnaean systems group organisms by shared features and relationships. Indigenous systems often go further by including the organism's role in the ecosystem, its seasonal behaviour, and its relationship to Country — dimensions the Linnaean system alone does not capture.
💬 Reflect
What might scientists learn by working with Wurundjeri knowledge holders when classifying a newly discovered organism on Country?
📚 Booklet p.8 · Year 8 extension
Year 8 Extension

Beyond kingdoms — the three domains

Modern DNA analysis shows that the six-kingdom system misses a deeper division. Scientists now recognise three domains above kingdom level:

  • Bacteria — single-celled, no nucleus (prokaryotic). Incredibly diverse.
  • Archaea — single-celled, no nucleus, but genetically very different from bacteria. Often found in extreme environments.
  • Eukarya — all organisms with cells that have a nucleus: animals, plants, fungi, and protists.
Why does this matter?
DNA sequencing revealed that Archaea are more closely related to Eukarya (including us!) than to Bacteria — a huge surprise. Classification must follow the evidence, not just physical appearance.
💬 Extension challenge
Which domain do YOU belong to? What evidence would you use to classify yourself using the three-domain system?
📚 Booklet p.8 · Year 8 extension
Year 8 Extension

How do we measure how closely related species are?

Shared classification levels tell us about relatedness. But modern biologists can go further using DNA comparison.

  • The more DNA sequences two species share, the more recently they shared a common ancestor
  • Humans and chimpanzees share ~98.7% of DNA
  • Humans and wombats share ~85% of DNA
  • Humans and bacteria share ~31% of DNA
Phylogenetic trees
A phylogenetic tree shows evolutionary relationships between species. Unlike a dichotomous key (which identifies), a phylogenetic tree shows how long ago species diverged from a shared ancestor.
💬 Think
The platypus is in the class Mammalia but lays eggs. Does shared DNA or shared features better capture how closely related the platypus is to a kangaroo?
Section 2 · Recap

What we covered

  • The Linnaean system has 7 levels: Kingdom, Phylum, Class, Order, Family, Genus, Species
  • Kingdom is broadest; Species is most specific
  • Organisms in the same level share more features the lower you go
  • The eastern grey kangaroo: Animalia → Chordata → Mammalia → Diprotodontia → Macropodidae → Macropus giganteus
  • Wurundjeri-Woiwurrung knowledge systems also group organisms by features and relationships — built over thousands of years of observation

Two organisms are in the same Family. Name THREE other classification levels they must also share. Which level are they guaranteed NOT to share?
