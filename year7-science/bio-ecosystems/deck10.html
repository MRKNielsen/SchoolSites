📚 Booklet p.31 · §10.1
Concept

Habitat loss and fragmentation

Victoria has lost over 66% of its native vegetation cover since European settlement. This is the leading cause of biodiversity loss in Victoria.

Fragmentation is often more damaging than equivalent clearing because:

  • Animals cannot move between habitat patches to find food, mates, or shelter
  • Genetic diversity declines as small isolated populations inbreed
  • The "edge effect" — altered conditions at the boundary of a patch (more wind, different temperature, invasive weeds) degrade habitat quality even inside the remaining patch
  • Local extinction from a patch cannot be reversed by re-colonisation if the patch is isolated
📚 Booklet p.32 · §10.2
Concept

Pollution — nutrient runoff and eutrophication

Eutrophication is the process where excess nutrients (nitrogen and phosphorus from fertilisers or sewage) enter a waterway, triggering rapid algal growth.

Victorian example: Gippsland Lakes
The Gippsland Lakes (the largest inland lake system in Australia) experience regular algal blooms caused by nutrient runoff from agriculture. Blue-green algal blooms produce toxins harmful to fish, waterbirds, pets, and humans. In 2007, the largest recorded bloom turned the lakes an iridescent blue-green visible from space.
💬 Predict
How would eutrophication in the Gippsland Lakes affect the local fishing industry (provisioning ecosystem service)? Trace the link from nutrient runoff to economic impact.
📚 Booklet p.33 · §10.3
Concept

Climate change — shifting conditions, shifting species

Victoria has warmed by approximately 1.0°C since 1910, with increased frequency of heatwaves, drought, and extreme fire weather.

Observed impacts on Victorian biodiversity:

  • Snow cover in the alps declining — mountain pygmy possum habitat shrinking
  • Species shifting southward and upslope to stay within their temperature range
  • Coral bleaching on Victorian sub-alpine reefs (rare deepwater corals)
  • Phenological mismatch — flowering earlier, but pollinators not yet active at same time
  • 2019–20 Black Summer fires burned ~1.5 million ha of Victoria — unprecedented for speed and intensity
Compounding effects
Climate change rarely acts alone. A species already stressed by habitat fragmentation and introduced predators is far less able to cope with additional climate pressure. The three threats together are far more damaging than any one alone — conservation must address all simultaneously.
💬 Analyse
The mountain pygmy possum faces: shrinking alpine snow cover, declining Bogong moth migration, and cat predation. Rank these threats in order of urgency and justify your ranking.
📚 Booklet p.33 · Q10.4
Work through with the class

Worked example — Leadbeater's possum: multiple threats

✍️ Analyse the cumulative impact of multiple threats
Leadbeater's possum (Gymnobelideus leadbeateri) is Victoria's critically endangered faunal emblem. It lives only in mountain ash forests of the Central Highlands. Analyse how multiple human impacts have pushed it to the brink of extinction.
  • Habitat loss: Logging of old-growth mountain ash has removed most trees old enough to develop the hollow limbs Leadbeater's possums require for nesting. Hollow formation takes 150+ years; logged trees are cut before hollows form.
  • Fire: The 2009 Black Saturday fires burned ~45% of remaining critical habitat. Mountain ash have a mast-seeding response to fire, but fire before hollows reform resets the clock to zero.
  • Climate change: Increasing fire frequency means mountains ash forests are burned more often, with less time to develop old-growth structure between fires. This compounds the effect of historical logging.
  • Conclusion: The cumulative effect of logging, fire, and climate change has reduced critical habitat by >90%. With <1,000 possums remaining, any further disturbance risks crossing an extinction threshold. Protecting remaining old-growth is the single most urgent conservation action.

First Nations perspectives

Long-term ecological memory — knowing what Country looked like before

Understanding the full scale of human impact on ecosystems requires knowing what they looked like before those impacts. For most of Victoria, written records only extend back about 190 years.

But Wurundjeri-Woiwurrung oral history, Songlines, and traditional ecological knowledge encode information about Country going back tens of thousands of years — including ecological baselines that scientists are only beginning to document.

🌎 Two-eyed seeing

The concept of Etuaptmumk (Two-Eyed Seeing), developed by Mi'kmaw Elder Albert Marshall, is now widely adopted in environmental science. It means holding two perspectives simultaneously — Indigenous knowledge and Western science — recognising both as valid and complementary.

In Victorian ecosystem management, Wurundjeri ecological knowledge is increasingly combined with scientific monitoring data to set more accurate biodiversity restoration targets. First Nations people know what a healthy Country looks and sounds like — scientists now work to document and support that.

📚 Booklet p.34 · Year 8 extension
Year 8 Extension

Minimum viable population — how small is too small?

A minimum viable population (MVP) is the smallest population size at which a species can sustain itself without suffering an unacceptable risk of extinction over a given period.

Small populations face additional risks:

  • Genetic diversity loss: Inbreeding increases. Harmful recessive alleles become more common. Disease resistance falls.
  • Demographic stochasticity: Random variation in birth/death rates can cause extinction in small populations even if the average rate would sustain a large one
  • Allee effect: Below a certain density, individuals cannot find mates — reproduction collapses
Victorian example
Eastern barred bandicoots were reduced to a single population of <100 animals at Hamilton, Victoria, by the 1980s. Captive breeding and reintroduction programs have brought them back — but genetic analysis shows the wild population still has very low diversity from that bottleneck. One novel disease could collapse the entire Victorian population.
📚 Booklet p.34 · Year 8 extension
Year 8 Extension

Planetary boundaries — safe operating limits for Earth

Scientists have proposed nine planetary boundaries — safe operating limits within which human civilisation can persist. Exceeding these boundaries risks triggering abrupt, large-scale changes to Earth systems.

Currently exceeded boundaries (2024):

  • Biosphere integrity (biodiversity loss) — well exceeded
  • Land-system change (habitat loss) — exceeded
  • Climate change — exceeded
  • Freshwater change — exceeded
  • Novel entities (pollution including microplastics) — exceeded
What this means
Biodiversity loss is not just an environmental issue — it is connected to every other planetary boundary. Healthy ecosystems regulate climate, purify water, prevent floods, and maintain soil fertility. Crossing the biodiversity boundary makes it harder to stay within all the others. This is why biodiversity conservation is increasingly framed as an urgent global priority, not just a conservation interest.
💬 Discuss
If you had to choose which planetary boundary was most urgent to address in Victoria, which would you choose and why? Is it possible to address them independently?
Section 10 · Recap

What we covered

  • The five major human impacts on ecosystems: habitat loss/fragmentation, pollution, introduced species, climate change, over-harvesting — these usually act together
  • Fragmentation is often more damaging than equivalent clearing — isolated patches cannot support viable populations long-term
  • Eutrophication: nutrient runoff → algal bloom → oxygen depletion → aquatic ecosystem collapse
  • Victorian species like Leadbeater's possum and the mountain pygmy possum face multiple compounding threats — addressing any one in isolation is insufficient
  • Wurundjeri ecological knowledge provides long-term baselines for understanding the true scale of ecosystem change

Choose ONE Victorian species that is threatened by human activity. Identify at least TWO different types of human impact affecting it and explain how they interact to make the threat worse than either alone.
