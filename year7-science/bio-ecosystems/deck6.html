Arrow rule
Arrows in a food chain show the direction of energy flow — they point FROM the food source TOWARD the consumer (the organism doing the eating).

Work through with the class

Worked example — what if rabbits disappeared?

✍️ Predict the effects of removing one species
Rabbits were removed from a Victorian woodland food web (e.g. by disease or predators). Rabbits eat native shrubs and are eaten by eastern quolls and wedge-tailed eagles. Predict the effects.
  • Effect on prey: Native shrubs would experience less grazing pressure — shrub populations would likely increase.
  • Effect on predators: Eastern quolls and eagles lose a food source. Quoll and eagle populations may decline unless they switch to other prey (e.g. crimson rosellas, ringtail possums).
  • Wider effects: If quolls decline, grasshoppers (a quoll food source) may increase, leading to more grass being eaten.
  • Conclusion: Removing one species sends ripple effects throughout the web. Complex webs are more resilient — there are alternative food sources. Simple chains collapse entirely.

Your turn

Now you try

✍️ Predict the effects of losing the wedge-tailed eagle
The wedge-tailed eagle is the apex predator in our woodland food web. If eagle populations collapsed due to habitat loss, predict TWO effects on other species in the web.
  • Effect 1: Secondary consumers (brown falcon, eastern quoll, lace monitor) experience less predation from above — their numbers may increase.
  • Effect 2: As secondary consumers increase, primary consumers (rabbits, kangaroos, rosellas) face more predation pressure — their numbers may decline.
  • Conclusion: The eagle acts as a keystone species — controlling populations below it. Its loss causes a cascade of changes (a "trophic cascade") through the entire web.

First Nations perspectives

Caring for Country — fire and food webs

Wurundjeri-Woiwurrung people used cultural burning — careful, seasonal, low-intensity burns — to manage the landscape on Country for thousands of years.

These burns were not random. They were timed using ecological knowledge: which animals were nesting, which plants were seeding, which areas needed renewal. The result was a mosaic of different vegetation ages across Country.

🌎 Effect on food webs
A mosaic landscape — patches of fresh growth alongside older bush — supports a greater variety of species at different trophic levels. Fresh grass attracts grazing animals; dense scrub provides cover for prey and nesting sites; open areas allow raptors to hunt. Cultural burning actively increased food web complexity and biodiversity.

Year 8 Extension

Trophic levels and energy pyramids

Each "feeding level" in a food chain is called a trophic level.

  • Level 1: Producers (plants, algae)
  • Level 2: Primary consumers (herbivores)
  • Level 3: Secondary consumers
  • Level 4+: Tertiary / apex consumers

Energy is lost at each level — as heat, movement, and waste. Only about 10% of energy is transferred from one level to the next.

Year 8 Extension

Keystone species — disproportionate importance

A keystone species has an outsized effect on its ecosystem relative to its abundance. Remove a keystone species and the ecosystem changes dramatically.

Australian examples:

  • Sea urchin (removed by otters in the Pacific) — when urchins explode in number, they destroy kelp forests
  • Bettongs — small marsupials that dig up fungal spores and aerate soil, supporting plant diversity across Australian woodlands
  • Wedge-tailed eagle — apex predator controlling herbivore populations
Trophic cascade
When a keystone species (usually an apex predator) is removed, the effects cascade down through all trophic levels — herbivores increase, plants decrease, other species change. This is called a trophic cascade.
💬 Extension challenge
In Yellowstone, USA, reintroducing wolves caused rivers to change course — because deer stopped overgrazing riverbanks. How is this an example of a trophic cascade?
Section 6 · Recap

What we covered

  • Producers make food from sunlight; consumers eat other organisms; decomposers break down dead matter
  • Arrows in food chains/webs point in the direction of energy flow (from food to consumer)
  • Food webs are more realistic than food chains — most organisms eat and are eaten by multiple species
  • Removing one species can send ripple effects through the whole web
  • Wurundjeri cultural burning created complex mosaic landscapes that supported richer, more stable food webs
