Concept

In-situ vs ex-situ conservation

Best practice
In-situ and ex-situ strategies work best in combination. Captive breeding keeps a species alive while in-situ habitat is restored. Reintroduction into protected wild habitat is the ultimate goal of most captive breeding programs.
📚 Booklet p.36 · Q1
Victorian conservation success

Eastern barred bandicoot — back from near-extinction

The eastern barred bandicoot (Perameles gunnii) was declared extinct in the wild in Victoria in 1989, with only ~150 animals surviving in one location at Hamilton.

Recovery program (1989–present):

  • Ex-situ: Captive breeding at Zoos Victoria (Melbourne Zoo, Healesville Sanctuary, Werribee Open Range Zoo)
  • In-situ: Predator-proof fenced enclosures at three sites; predator control at additional reintroduction sites
  • Outcome: Wild population in Victoria now over 1,500 — reclassified from "extinct in the wild" to "endangered" in 2022
Key lesson
The eastern barred bandicoot recovery shows that near-extinction is not always irreversible — but it requires sustained investment, both in-situ and ex-situ strategies working together, and removal of the threats (predators) that caused the decline. Without fox and cat control, reintroduction fails.
💬 Evaluate
The bandicoot recovery took over 30 years and required zoos, government funding, and private landholders. Is this level of investment justified for one species? What arguments would you make for and against?

Work through with the class

Worked example — evaluating habitat corridors

✍️ Evaluate a conservation strategy with evidence
The Victorian government has invested in wildlife corridors — strips of native vegetation linking isolated habitat patches across farmland. Evaluate the effectiveness of this strategy.
  • How it works: Corridors allow animals to move between habitat patches to find food, mates, and shelter. They reduce the genetic isolation that leads to inbreeding depression in small populations.
  • Evidence for effectiveness: Studies of Victorian woodland birds show that species richness in patches connected by corridors is significantly higher than in isolated equivalent patches. Powerful owls track corridors in suburban Melbourne.
  • Limitations: Corridors can also act as conduits for introduced species, fire, and disease. They require landholders' cooperation. Maintaining corridor vegetation is an ongoing cost. Some species won't use corridors if they are too narrow or contain unsuitable vegetation.
  • Conclusion: Habitat corridors are a well-evidenced and cost-effective in-situ strategy, particularly effective for mobile species like birds. For less mobile species (some reptiles, frogs), they must be supplemented with population management within patches. Not a single solution, but a vital component of landscape-scale conservation.

Your turn

Now you try — design a conservation action

✍️ Design and evaluate a local conservation action
Northcote / the inner north of Melbourne has patches of native vegetation in parks and creek corridors. Design ONE conservation action your school or community could realistically take. Evaluate it using the framework: What? How does it help? What are the limitations?
  • Example action — school native garden: Plant indigenous species (local provenance) in the school grounds. Focus on species that provide food and shelter for local birds and insects.
  • How it helps: Adds habitat within a fragmented urban landscape. Provides a stepping stone for birds and insects moving between Merri Creek corridor and other green patches. Increases local species richness and raises student ecological literacy.
  • Limitations: Small area — limited impact on population viability for any species. Introduced weed species nearby may colonise the garden if not managed. Must use local provenance (genetic material from the same region) for ecological benefit.
  • Your turn: Think of your own action. Write it in your booklet using the same three-part framework: What? How does it help? Limitations?

First Nations perspectives

Caring for Country — the world's oldest conservation system

Biodiversity conservation is often framed as a modern scientific endeavour. But First Nations peoples across Australia — including Wurundjeri-Woiwurrung — have been managing Country for ecosystem health for at least 65,000 years.

Increasingly, conservation outcomes improve dramatically when Wurundjeri-Woiwurrung knowledge holders and land managers are co-designing and leading conservation alongside scientists — not just consulted at the margins.

🌎 Indigenous Protected Areas and Country Plans

Across Australia, Indigenous Protected Areas (IPAs) are managed by First Nations communities and now cover over 67 million hectares — the largest single category of protected area in Australia. Studies consistently show that biodiversity outcomes in Country managed by First Nations peoples rival or exceed those in equivalent areas managed by conservation agencies.

For Wurundjeri-Woiwurrung people, the return of land and decision-making authority over Country is not just an environmental matter — it is about self-determination and healing for community, culture, and Country together.

Year 8 Extension

Legal protection — EPBC Act and international frameworks

The Environment Protection and Biodiversity Conservation Act 1999 (EPBC Act) is Australia's main federal environmental law. It:

  • Lists threatened species and ecological communities (critical habitat)
  • Requires environmental assessment for any action that could significantly impact a listed species
  • Establishes recovery plans for critically endangered species

Limitations: A 2020 review by Professor Graeme Samuel found the EPBC Act "is not fit for purpose" — species have continued to decline despite listing. Enforcement is inconsistent; economic development is frequently approved that would affect listed habitat.

International: Convention on Biological Diversity
The CBD (agreed 1992) commits member nations to conserving biodiversity and using it sustainably. The 2022 Kunming-Montreal Agreement set a target of protecting 30% of land and ocean by 2030 ("30x30"). Australia signed but as of 2024, only ~13% of Australia's land is formally protected, and protection quality varies greatly.

Year 8 Extension

Rewilding — letting ecosystems restore themselves

Rewilding is a conservation approach that focuses on restoring the processes that drive ecosystem recovery, rather than managing individual species. It often involves reintroducing keystone species (especially apex predators) so natural trophic cascades can operate.

Australian examples:

  • Rewilding Australia: Large fenced sanctuaries (Arid Recovery, Wild Deserts) where cats and foxes are removed and lost native mammals reintroduced
  • Dingo rewilding: Evidence that dingo presence suppresses fox and cat populations, allowing smaller native mammals to recover — but controversial with farming communities
Victorian potential
Victoria is exploring large-scale rewilding in areas like the East Gippsland forests damaged by the 2019–20 fires. Quolls, bettongs, and bandicoots have been reintroduced to some predator-free areas of Victoria with significant early success.
💬 Debate
Rewilding with apex predators (like bringing Tasmanian devils to mainland Victoria) is controversial. What would be the ecological arguments for? What concerns would farmers and communities have? Who should make this decision?
