Case study

Mountain ash forests of the Central Highlands

Victoria's Central Highlands mountain ash (Eucalyptus regnans) forests are among the most productive ecosystems on Earth. They provide multiple ecosystem services simultaneously.

  • Provisioning: Clean drinking water for ~70% of Melbourne's supply (catchments)
  • Regulating: Store more carbon per hectare than tropical rainforest
  • Supporting: Habitat for Leadbeater's possum (critically endangered), gang-gang cockatoos, powerful owls
  • Cultural: Recreational hiking, tourism, Aboriginal cultural heritage
Threat — bushfire and logging
The 2009 Black Saturday fires and decades of logging have reduced old-growth mountain ash by over 90%. Old trees contain hollows that are critical for Leadbeater's possum nesting — lose the old trees and you lose the possum. This shows how threats to one species ripple through ecosystem services.
💬 Analyse
If mountain ash forests were logged, which of Melbourne's ecosystem services would be most immediately at risk? Rank the four categories in order of impact and justify your ranking.
📚 Booklet p.24 · Q7.3
Work through with the class

Worked example — what if pollinators disappeared?

✍️ Analyse the loss of a regulating service
Native bee populations in Victoria have declined due to habitat loss and pesticide use. Native bees pollinate native plants and many crops. Predict the knock-on effects of a 50% decline in native bee populations.
  • Direct effect — provisioning: Crop yields for pollinator-dependent plants (almonds, apples, blueberries) would decrease significantly. Food prices increase.
  • Direct effect — supporting: Native plant reproduction decreases. Fewer native flowers means less habitat and food for insects and birds that depend on them.
  • Food web effect: Insects and birds that eat nectar or seeds decline. Their predators also decline — a trophic cascade.
  • Conclusion: The loss of a single regulating service (pollination) cascades into provisioning, supporting, and cultural services. Biodiversity loss and ecosystem service loss are deeply linked.

📚 Booklet p.24 · Q7.4
Your turn

Now you try — Birrarung (Yarra River)

✍️ Analyse ecosystem services for a Victorian waterway
The Birrarung (Yarra River) runs through Melbourne. It is culturally significant to Wurundjeri-Woiwurrung people and provides services to millions of Melburnians. Identify ONE ecosystem service from EACH category that the Birrarung provides.
  • Provisioning: Fresh water for drinking, irrigation, and fishing — e.g. native fish such as Murray cod were a traditional food source.
  • Regulating: Riparian vegetation along its banks filters pollutants, slows floodwaters, and reduces erosion.
  • Cultural: Deep spiritual and cultural significance to Wurundjeri-Woiwurrung people; recreational swimming, kayaking, and festivals for all Melburnians.
  • Supporting: Provides habitat for platypus, rakali (native water rat), and many waterbirds. These species maintain the river ecosystem that provides all other services.

📚 Booklet p.24
First Nations perspectives

Caring for Country protects ecosystem services

For Wurundjeri-Woiwurrung people, Caring for Country is an obligation and a relationship — not just environmental management. Country includes the land, water, plants, animals, and spiritual elements as an interconnected whole.

Practices of Caring for Country — including cultural burning, managing water flows, and protecting sacred sites — maintained ecosystem services for tens of thousands of years. Colonisation disrupted these practices, and many services have since degraded.

🌎 Caring for Country as ecosystem service management

Cultural burning maintained open grasslands and forest edges (provisioning — food; supporting — habitat). Strict laws about not over-harvesting maintained fish and plant populations (provisioning). Seasonal movement across Country ensured ecosystems were not over-exploited in any one area.

Modern conservation science is increasingly recognising that Country managed by First Nations peoples often has higher biodiversity and more intact ecosystem services than areas managed by other methods.

📚 Booklet p.24 · Year 8 extension
Year 8 Extension

Natural capital — putting a dollar value on nature

Economists use the term natural capital to describe the stock of natural resources and ecosystems that provide services to humans.

In 2023, a global study estimated the total value of ecosystem services worldwide at US$125 trillion per year — nearly 1.5 times global GDP.

  • Victoria's Yarra Ranges (mountain ash) catchment: valued at AU$11 billion in water services alone
  • Great Barrier Reef (shared with NSW/QLD): AU$6.4 billion/year in tourism and fisheries
  • Victorian wetlands: filter billions of litres of water — worth more than equivalent water treatment plants
Limitations of dollar valuation
Not all ecosystem services can be priced. How do you put a dollar value on spiritual connection to Country, or on the intrinsic right of an organism to exist? Many ecologists and Indigenous peoples argue that reducing nature to economic value misses its deeper importance.
💬 Debate
Do you think putting a dollar value on ecosystem services helps protect them, or does it risk reducing nature to a commodity? What might be lost in both approaches?

Year 8 Extension

Tipping points — when ecosystems stop functioning

Ecosystems can absorb some disturbance and bounce back — this is called resilience. But if stress exceeds a threshold (a "tipping point"), the ecosystem can shift permanently to a new state that provides far fewer services.

Examples:

  • Coral reef: bleaching events → algae dominance → no longer supports fish, tourism, or coastal protection
  • Wetland: nutrient runoff → algal bloom → oxygen depletion → collapse of aquatic ecosystem
  • Grassland: overgrazing → soil compaction → erosion → loss of provisioning services
Section 7 · Recap

What we covered

  • Ecosystem services are the benefits humans receive from healthy ecosystems — sorted into provisioning, regulating, cultural, and supporting categories
  • Victorian examples include Melbourne's water supply from mountain ash forests, pollination of food crops, and the Birrarung's role in water purification
  • Biodiversity loss threatens ecosystem services — losing one species or one service can cascade through all categories
  • Wurundjeri-Woiwurrung Caring for Country practices maintained ecosystem services for tens of thousands of years; modern conservation recognises their effectiveness
💬 Exit question
Choose ONE ecosystem service that you personally benefit from. Explain which category it belongs to, what organisms or processes make it possible, and what would happen if biodiversity in that ecosystem declined.
