Key word
Ecosystem: all the living things (biotic) and non-living things (abiotic) in an area, and all the interactions between them.
📚 Booklet p.15 · §5.1
Concept

What is an ecosystem?

An ecosystem is a community of living organisms (biotic factors) interacting with each other and with their non-living environment (abiotic factors).

  • Biotic factors: plants, animals, fungi, bacteria, other organisms
  • Abiotic factors: temperature, rainfall, sunlight, soil type, wind, water, pH
  • Ecosystems can be tiny (a pond, a rotting log) or vast (the Great Barrier Reef, the Murray-Darling Basin)
  • Living and non-living components constantly interact — change one, and others are affected
Example: a Victorian pond
Biotic: water plants, frogs, mosquito larvae, algae, fish, microbes
Abiotic: water temperature, pH, oxygen levels, sunlight, sediment depth, rainfall
💬 Think
If rainfall in a Victorian woodland dropped by 50% for three years, name TWO ways this abiotic change might affect biotic factors in the ecosystem.

Concept

What is biodiversity?

Biodiversity is the variety of life on Earth — at three levels:

  • Genetic diversity: variety of genes within a species (e.g. wombats in Victoria vs Queensland have slightly different genes)
  • Species diversity: number of different species in an area
  • Ecosystem diversity: variety of different ecosystem types

Australia has some of the highest biodiversity in the world, including about 87% of mammal species, 93% of reptile species, and 45% of bird species found nowhere else on Earth.

High vs Low biodiversity
High biodiversity: many species interacting — generally more stable, more resilient to change.

Low biodiversity: few species — more vulnerable to disruption (e.g. a single crop field if one pest arrives).
💬 Think
A wheat paddock has one plant species. A nearby remnant woodland has 60 plant species and 120 animal species. Which has higher biodiversity? What might happen to each if a new fungal disease attacked wheat?

Worked example — comparing two areas

✍️ Which area has higher biodiversity, and why?
Area A: a remnant box-ironbark woodland with 52 plant species, 23 mammal species, and 140 bird species. Area B: a plantation pine forest with 1 tree species, 3 mammal species, and 12 bird species.
  • Count species diversity: Area A has 52 + 23 + 140 = 215 species. Area B has 1 + 3 + 12 = 16 species.
  • Compare: Area A has far more species. It also has a complex layered structure (canopy, shrubs, ground layer). Area B is monoculture — one tree species dominates.
  • Area A has significantly higher biodiversity. It also has greater stability — if one species is lost, many others can fill its role. Area B is fragile — a single pest could collapse the entire plantation.

First Nations perspectives

Understanding biodiversity through Country

Wurundjeri-Woiwurrung people recognise and name distinct parts of Country — the high country (ngarung), river country (Birrarung), grasslands, coastal areas — each with its own character, species, and seasonal patterns.

This is an understanding of ecosystem diversity developed over 40,000+ years. It includes detailed knowledge of which species live where, why, and how they interact — a form of biodiversity mapping that predates Western ecology by millennia.

🌎 Biocultural diversity
Scientists now recognise that areas with high biological diversity often also have high cultural and linguistic diversity — because people living close to nature over long periods develop rich knowledge of it. Protecting biodiversity and protecting Indigenous language and culture are often connected goals.

📚 Booklet p.17 · Q5.4
Your turn

Now you try

✍️ Describe the biodiversity of a Victorian alpine ecosystem
A mountain pygmy possum, a corroboree frog, alpine ash trees, snow daisies, and a kookaburra are all found in Victoria's alpine zone. Ice, wind, and very cold winters are key abiotic factors. Answer: (a) Name two biotic and two abiotic factors. (b) Is this high or low biodiversity? (c) What might happen if warming temperatures reduced snow cover?
  • (a) Biotic: mountain pygmy possum, corroboree frog, alpine ash tree, snow daisies. Abiotic: ice/snow, wind, cold temperatures, short growing season.
  • (b) This is high biodiversity — many species have adapted to these specific conditions, and many are found nowhere else.
  • (c) Reduced snow cover would alter abiotic conditions, threatening species adapted to cold and snow (including the pygmy possum, which relies on snowpack for insulation during hibernation). Introduced plant species might also invade as conditions warm.

Year 8 Extension

Beyond Victoria — Australia's ecosystem diversity

Australia contains some of the world's most distinctive ecosystems — shaped by millions of years of isolation.

  • Tropical rainforest (Daintree, QLD) — highest plant biodiversity in Australia; ancient lineages including 25% of all Australian frog species
  • Arid desert (Great Victoria Desert) — adapted to extreme heat and drought; sand goannas, bilbies, spinifex grass
  • Tropical savanna (NT/WA) — seasonal wet/dry cycles; quolls, wallabies, freshwater crocodiles
  • Mallee scrubland (northwest Victoria) — fire-adapted, biodiverse, home to mallee fowl and hundreds of reptile species
Why Australia is unique
Australia separated from other landmasses ~50 million years ago. With no land connection, species evolved in isolation — producing unique marsupials, monotremes, and plants found nowhere else. This is called endemism.
💬 Extension challenge
Compare the Daintree rainforest and the Victorian alpine ecosystem. Which would you expect to have higher species diversity, and why?

Measuring biodiversity — the Simpson Index

Simply counting species (species richness) doesn't tell the whole story. A forest with 100 eucalyptus trees and 1 wattle is less diverse than one with 50 of each.

The Simpson Diversity Index accounts for both richness (number of species) AND evenness (how equally distributed they are).

Formula concept
D = 1 minus the probability that two randomly chosen organisms belong to the same species.
D near 1 = high diversity, D near 0 = low diversity.
💬 Example
Area A: 40 wattles, 40 she-oaks, 40 grasses (even distribution).
Area B: 115 wattles, 3 she-oaks, 2 grasses (uneven).

Both have the same number of species (3). Which has higher biodiversity? Why does evenness matter?

What we covered
