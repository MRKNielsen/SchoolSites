Concept

Rules of binomial nomenclature

Every species gets a two-part Latin name made up of its Genus and species.

  • First word: Genus — always capitalised
  • Second word: species — always lower case
  • Both words are italicised when typed (or underlined when handwritten)
  • Based in Latin or Greek — used globally regardless of local language

Worked example — Which kingdom?

✍️ Use features to assign kingdoms
For each organism below, name the kingdom and give two reasons for your choice:
(A) a mushroom
(B) a wattle tree
(C) Salmonella bacteria
  • (A) Mushroom
  • Multicellular, eukaryotic, cannot photosynthesise, decomposes dead material. Kingdom: Fungi
  • (B) Wattle tree (Acacia)
  • Multicellular, eukaryotic, makes its own food via photosynthesis, has leaves, stems, and roots. Kingdom: Plantae
  • (C) Salmonella
  • Unicellular, NO nucleus (prokaryotic), absorbs nutrients. This rules out all kingdoms with a nucleus. Kingdom: Bacteria

✍️ Classify each organism into its kingdom
For each organism, write the kingdom and ONE key reason:
(A) An amoeba — unicellular with a nucleus, consumes other cells for nutrients
(B) A common wombat — multicellular, eats food
(C) A species found living in Yellowstone hot springs at 80°C
  • (A) Amoeba

  • (B) Common wombat
  • Multicellular, eukaryotic, eats other organisms. Kingdom: Animalia
  • (C) Hot spring organism
  • Lives in extreme heat, unicellular, prokaryotic (no nucleus). Kingdom: Archaea — the domain of extremophiles.

First Nations perspectives

Species names: language and knowledge

Wurundjeri-Woiwurrung language contains specific names for organisms that encode ecological knowledge — not just labels, but information about relationships, behaviours, and uses.

For example, Wurundjeri names for plants often reflect their seasonal appearance, their use as food or medicine, or their relationship to animals that depend on them.

This is knowledge produced by living on Country for over 40,000 years — far longer than Western science has studied these ecosystems.

🌱 Example — the name "Wurundjeri"
wurun = the manna gum (Eucalyptus viminalis), the white gum along the Yarra · djeri = the grub found in that tree. The name itself records a relationship between a plant and the animal that depends on it.
🌎 Why this matters
When Linnaeus's system was developed, it gave every species one Latin name — overwriting existing Indigenous names. Today, scientists increasingly work with First Nations communities to ensure that Indigenous names and knowledge are acknowledged alongside — and not replaced by — scientific nomenclature.

Year 8 Extension

Eukaryotic vs Prokaryotic cells

Prokaryotic (Bacteria & Archaea)

  • No true nucleus — DNA floats freely in the cell
  • No membrane-bound organelles
  • Smallest and most ancient cell type
  • Examples: E. coli, Streptococcus, hot-spring Archaea

Eukaryotic (Animalia, Plantae, Fungi, Protista)

  • DNA enclosed in a membrane-bound nucleus
  • Contains specialised organelles (mitochondria, etc.)
  • Generally larger and more complex
  • Examples: human cells, plant cells, amoeba, yeast
Memory device
Prokaryote = Professional early bird — they were here first (no nucleus).
Eukaryote = Euphoric nucleus — they have a "true" (eu = good/true) nucleus.
📚 Booklet p.11 · Year 8 extension
Year 8 Extension

Autotrophs and heterotrophs

Autotroph
Self-feeder — makes its own food from simple molecules using an energy source (usually sunlight). Example: all plants, algae, and some bacteria.

Also called: producers
Heterotroph
Other-feeder — must consume other organisms to get energy. Example: all animals, fungi, and most bacteria.

Also called: consumers (or decomposers if breaking down dead matter)
💬 Extension challenge
The Venus flytrap is a plant (Plantae) that also traps and digests insects. Is it an autotroph, heterotroph, or both? What does this tell us about the limits of simple categories?
Section 3 · Recap

What we covered

  • Binomial nomenclature: every species gets one two-part name — Genus (capital) + species (lowercase), both italicised
  • Scientific names are used globally so scientists everywhere can communicate clearly
  • There are six kingdoms: Animalia, Plantae, Fungi, Protista, Bacteria, Archaea
  • We classify organisms into kingdoms using: unicellular/multicellular, eukaryotic/prokaryotic, how they get energy
  • Indigenous names encode ecological knowledge developed over thousands of years and deserve acknowledgement alongside scientific nomenclature
