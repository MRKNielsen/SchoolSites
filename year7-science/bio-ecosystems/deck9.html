Vocabulary to know
Native — evolved in this ecosystem over long periods. Introduced (exotic) — brought by humans from another region. Invasive — introduced species that spreads rapidly and causes ecological harm. Not all introduced species become invasive.

Victorian case study

Red fox — Victoria's most damaging introduced predator

The European red fox (Vulpes vulpes) was introduced to Victoria in the 1860s for recreational hunting. It spread rapidly across the continent.

Why is the fox so effective?

  • Opportunistic omnivore — eats almost anything
  • Native prey (bandicoots, potoroos, bettongs, ground-nesting birds) evolved with no fox-like predators — they have no effective escape behaviours
  • Breeds quickly: 4–6 kits per year
  • Adapts to urban and rural environments

Impact: Linked to the decline or extinction of over 20 native Australian mammal species.

Management: 1080 poison baiting
Sodium fluoroacetate (1080) occurs naturally in many Australian native plants. Native herbivores evolved some tolerance to it; introduced species like foxes and feral cats have very low tolerance. Baiting programs reduce fox populations, allowing native species to recover. This is used around Victoria's nationally important biodiversity zones.
💬 Analyse
Why do native Australian animals have such poor defensive responses to foxes compared to, say, European rabbits? Think about what each species evolved alongside.

Worked example — European carp in Victorian rivers

✍️ Trace an introduced species impact chain
European carp (Cyprinus carpio) were introduced to Australia in the 1960s and now dominate many Victorian river systems including the Murray-Darling Basin. Trace the chain of ecological effects.
  • Direct action: Carp feed by sucking up sediment from the river bottom and spitting out plant material. This disturbs sediment and uproots aquatic plants.
  • Water clarity: Disturbed sediment makes the water turbid (murky). Reduced light penetration means fewer aquatic plants can photosynthesise.
  • Ecosystem cascade: Fewer aquatic plants = less oxygen produced = oxygen depletion. Native fish, macroinvertebrates, and platypus all suffer. Food webs collapse.
  • Conclusion: One introduced species, through its feeding behaviour, degrades water quality, removes plant habitat, and collapses the food web supporting dozens of native species. Carp now constitute up to 90% of fish biomass in some Victorian waterways.

Your turn

Now you try — feral cats

✍️ Analyse the impact of feral cats in Victoria
Feral cats (Felis catus) are estimated to kill over 1.5 billion native animals per year across Australia. In Victoria they are active in forests, grasslands, and alpine environments. Identify the mechanism of harm and trace one impact chain.
  • Mechanism: Predation — feral cats are highly effective hunters, active at night, taking birds, small mammals, reptiles, frogs, and invertebrates.
  • Impact on small mammals: Eastern barred bandicoot, southern brown bandicoot, and other small ground-dwelling mammals face intense nocturnal predation. These species evolved alongside quolls, not cats; they have limited anti-cat escape responses.
  • Cascade: Decline of bandicoots reduces soil disturbance (bandicoots dig for grubs, aerating soil). Soil health and seed germination decline, reducing plant diversity.
  • Management challenge: Cats are legally pets in urban areas — distinguishing feral from domestic is difficult. Predator-proof fencing around small reserves (e.g. Woodlands Historic Park) is one Victorian strategy.

First Nations perspectives

Country before and after — Wurundjeri perspectives on ecological change

Before European settlement in 1835, Wurundjeri-Woiwurrung Country was managed for tens of thousands of years. The landscape Wurundjeri people managed supported extraordinary biodiversity — bettongs, quolls, bandicoots, and bush stone-curlews were common across Country.

European colonisation brought sheep, cattle, rabbits, foxes, cats, and blackberry almost simultaneously. Within decades, species that had persisted for millennia became locally extinct. This was not just an environmental event — it was the destruction of food sources, cultural sites, and relationships that were integral to Wurundjeri life on Country.

🌎 Country as relationship, not just habitat

For Wurundjeri-Woiwurrung people, animals on Country are not simply ecological components. They hold cultural significance, appear in Songlines and Dreaming stories, and are connected to specific family groups through totemic relationships.

The loss of bettongs, for example, is not only an ecological problem — it is the loss of a cultural relationship. This is why Wurundjeri Elders today are actively involved in ecological restoration projects: returning species to Country is also restoring cultural connections.

Year 8 Extension

Biological control — fighting introduced species with biology

Biological control uses living organisms to control invasive species — instead of chemicals or physical removal.

  • Myxomatosis (1950s): Rabbit-specific virus released in Australia — initially killed 99% of rabbits. Rabbits evolved partial resistance; myxoma virus evolved to be less lethal. Now used alongside calicivirus for ongoing control
  • Cane beetle grub control: The cane toad was introduced in 1935 to eat cane beetles — but it failed (can't reach beetles in the cane) and itself became one of Australia's worst invasive species
  • Carp herpesvirus: Currently being trialled — a carp-specific virus that could collapse carp populations in Australian waterways
Key risk of biological control
Introducing a biological control agent is itself introducing a new species. If the agent attacks native species as well as the target, the "cure" can be worse than the disease. The cane toad is the most famous example of a biological control gone wrong — now classified as one of the world's worst invasive species.

Year 8 Extension

Why islands (and island-like habitats) are most vulnerable

Species on isolated islands — or isolated habitat patches that act like islands — are especially vulnerable to introduced species because:

  • Small population sizes mean even modest predation rates can exceed reproduction
  • Island species often evolved in the absence of mammalian predators — they have no fear response to foxes or cats
  • No nearby population to re-colonise if local extinction occurs

Australia itself is effectively an island — its unique fauna evolved in isolation from the rest of the world's placental mammal predators.

Victorian example: Phillip Island
Little penguins on Phillip Island were devastated by foxes and dogs. After an intensive fox eradication program and dog restrictions, penguin numbers recovered from ~3,000 to over 40,000 in three decades. The island's physical isolation allowed eradication to be maintained — a luxury not available on the mainland.
💬 Discuss
How might a predator-proof fenced reserve act as an "island" for native species on the mainland? What are the advantages and limitations of this approach?
Section 9 · Recap

What we covered

  • Native species evolved here; introduced species were brought by humans; invasive introduced species spread and cause ecological harm
  • Introduced species harm ecosystems through predation, competition, habitat change, and disease — often by exploiting the fact that native species evolved without them
  • Victorian examples: red fox (predation of small mammals), European carp (water quality and food web collapse), feral cats (nocturnal predation of birds and mammals)
  • The ecological disruption caused by introduced species since 1835 also represents the destruction of Wurundjeri-Woiwurrung cultural relationships with Country
