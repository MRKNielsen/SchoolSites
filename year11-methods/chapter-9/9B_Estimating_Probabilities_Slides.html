01 · When counting fails

Relative frequency — probability from data

Relative frequency

Toss a drawing pin: is "point up" equally likely as "point down"? No reason it should be. So we experiment, and estimate:

Pr(A) ≈ number of times A occursnumber of trials (for a large number of trials)

Three kinds of probability

  • Subjective — a judgement call ("60% chance we win the Ashes"). Weakest.
  • From data — relative frequency over many trials. This lesson.
  • From a model — equally likely outcomes (9A), or areas (later today).
estimate after each batch of tosseslong-run value
02 · Worked example

I Do Example 1

Katia tosses a drawing pin 50 times and observes 33 ‘point up’. Mikki tosses the same pin 100 times and observes 62 ‘point up’.

  1. a What is Katia's estimate of Pr(point up)?
  2. b What is Mikki's estimate?
  3. c Which is the preferred estimate, and why?
  4. d What is the best estimate using all the information available?
  • aPr(point up) ≈ 3350 = 0.66.
  • bPr(point up) ≈ 62100 = 0.62.
  • cMikki's — her estimate uses more trials (100 vs 50), so it should be closer to the true probability.
  • dCombine the data to maximise trials: 95 point-up in 150 tosses, so Pr ≈ 95150 ≈ 0.63.
03 · Comparing estimates

We Do Example 2

Two misshapen dice are tested. Die A is thrown 400 times and shows a six 70 times. Die B is thrown 600 times and shows a six 93 times.

  1. a Estimate Pr(six) for each die.
  2. b If you want to throw a six, which die should you choose?
  • 1Die A: Pr(six) ≈ 70400 = 0.175.
  • 2Die B: Pr(six) ≈ 93600 = 0.155.
  • 3Choose die A — its estimated probability of a six is higher. (Note both differ from the fair-die value 1/6 ≈ 0.167.)

Compare rates, not counts

Die B produced more sixes (93 vs 70) — but from far more throws. Raw counts mislead; always divide by the number of trials.

Estimates vary

Repeat the experiment and you'll get slightly different numbers. That variation is normal — it shrinks as trials grow, which is why we prefer more trials.

05 · Probabilities from area

I Do Example 3

A square dartboard of side 12 cm has a red square of side 8 cm inside it. A dart is equally likely to land anywhere on the board. Find the probability it lands in the red area.
Probability from area

If every point is equally likely to be hit,

Pr(hit region) = area of regiontotal area
  • 1Total area: 122 = 144 cm2.
  • 2Red area: 82 = 64 cm2.
  • 3Pr(red) = 64144 = 49.

Same idea as counting

It's still favourable ÷ total — areas replace counts when the outcomes form a continuous region.

06 · Your turn

You Do Example 4

a. A spinner lands on ‘ten’ 24 times in 300 spins. Estimate Pr(ten).
Pr(ten) ≈ 24300 = 0.08.
b. In a survey of 2000 people, 890 regularly use social media. Estimate the probability the next person surveyed does too.
Pr ≈ 8902000 = 0.445.
c. A square of side 1 m contains a quarter-circle of radius 1 m centred at one corner. Find the probability a random dart in the square lands inside the quarter-circle.
Quarter-circle area = π(1)24; square area 1. Pr = π4 ≈ 0.785.
d. A circular board of radius 14 cm has an inner circle of radius 7 cm. Find the probability a random dart hits the outer ring.
Ring area = π(142 − 72) = 147π; total 196π. Pr = 147196 = 34.
07 · Using the TI-Nspire CX II CAS

CAS skills Simulating an experiment with randInt

The calculator can run hundreds of trials in a second — a fast way to estimate a probability by simulation.

Steps

  • In a Calculator page: menu ▸ Probability ▸ Random ▸ Integer.
  • randInt(1, 6, 60) simulates 60 die rolls. Store: randInt(1, 6, 60) → rolls.
  • Count sixes: countIf(rolls, 6), then divide by 60 for the relative frequency.

Try it

Run it three times. You'll get three different counts — each a legitimate estimate of 1/6 ≈ 0.167. Increase 60 to 600: the estimates cluster tighter. That's the law of large numbers on your screen.

08 · Summary 9B

Estimating probabilities — what to remember

From data

Pr(A) ≈ times A occurrednumber of trials
  • More trials → a more reliable estimate.
  • To combine experiments, add the counts and add the trials.

From area

Pr = favourable areatotal area when every point is equally likely.
All the 9A rules still hold: Pr ≥ 0, total is 1, complements work.

The #1 trap

Believing a short run must "balance out" — after five heads in a row, tails is not "due". The coin has no memory; only the long run settles towards the true probability.

Check · Mini-whiteboards

Check Hinge question — everyone shows an answer

On your whiteboard write A, B, C or D. On the count of three, hold it up — everyone answers at once.
A fair coin has just landed heads five times in a row. The probability that the next toss is a head is:
A  exactly 12
B  less than 12 — tails is due
C  more than 12 — heads is on a streak
D  132
Answer: A — exactly 1/2. Each toss of a fair coin is unaffected by the past. B is the gambler's fallacy ("due"); C is the streak fallacy; D is the probability of five heads in advance — not the probability of the next toss.
