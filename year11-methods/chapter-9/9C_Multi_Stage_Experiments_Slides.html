01 · Tree diagrams

I Do Example 1

A fair coin is tossed twice. Find the probability that: a one head is observed b at least one head is observed c both tosses give the same result.
Tree diagram

One stage per column; read along the branches to list every outcome. Here ε = {HH, HT, TH, TT} — four equally likely outcomes.

  • a‘One head’ = {HT, TH}: Pr = 24 = 12.
  • b‘At least one head’ = {HH, HT, TH}: Pr = 34.
  • c‘Same result’ = {HH, TT}: Pr = 24 = 12.

HT and TH are different

‘One head’ happens two ways. Merging HT and TH is the most common listing error.

02 · Tables for two-stage experiments

I Do Example 2

Two fair dice are rolled. Find the probability that: a a double is rolled b the sum is greater than 10.
Table of ordered pairs

Two dice → a 6 × 6 table: 36 equally likely outcomes (die 1, die 2).

  • aDoubles lie on the diagonal: {(1,1), (2,2), …, (6,6)} — 6 outcomes. Pr = 636 = 16.
  • bSum > 10: {(5,6), (6,5), (6,6)} — 3 outcomes. Pr = 336 = 112.
  • ✓Both answers come from counting cells — the table guarantees nothing is missed or double-counted.

04 · Three stages

We Do Example 3

A fair coin is tossed three times. Find the probability that: a exactly one head is observed b at least one head is observed c the second toss is a head.
  • 1The tree gives 2 × 2 × 2 = 8 equally likely outcomes.
  • a‘Exactly one head’ = {HTT, THT, TTH}: Pr = 38.
  • bComplement shortcut: ‘no heads’ = {TTT}, so Pr = 1 − 18 = 78.
  • c‘Second toss a head’ = {HHH, HHT, THH, THT}: Pr = 48 = 12.

"At least one" → complement

Count the one outcome you don't want instead of the seven you do.

05 · Mixed experiments

We Do Example 4

An experiment consists of tossing a coin and then rolling a die.

  1. a Use a tree diagram to list the sample space.
  2. b Find the probability of a tail together with a number greater than 4.
  • 1Stage 1 has 2 branches (H, T); each splits into 6 (1–6): 2 × 6 = 12 equally likely outcomes: H1, H2, …, T6.
  • 2‘Tail and number > 4’ = {T5, T6} — 2 outcomes.
  • 3Pr = 212 = 16.

Multiply the branch counts

Stages with m and n outcomes give m × n outcomes altogether — a preview of the multiplication principle in Chapter 10.

Keep stages in order

H3 means "head, then 3". Writing outcomes in a fixed stage order is what keeps the list complete.

06 · Your turn

You Do Example 5

a. Two dice are rolled. Find Pr(sum is even).
18 of the 36 cells have an even sum: Pr = 1836 = 12.
b. Two dice are rolled. Find Pr(sum ≤ 4).
{(1,1), (1,2), (2,1), (1,3), (3,1), (2,2)} — 6 cells: Pr = 636 = 16.
c. Three coins are tossed. Find Pr(exactly two tails).
{TTH, THT, HTT}: Pr = 38.
d. A café offers 2 entrées, 3 mains and 3 desserts. How many different three-course meals are possible? If all are equally likely, find Pr(a particular meal).
2 × 3 × 3 = 18 meals; each has probability 118.
07 · Using the TI-Nspire CX II CAS

CAS skills Simulating two dice

Check the table's prediction: simulate many rolls of two dice and compare relative frequency with n/36.

Steps

  • randInt(1, 6, 100) + randInt(1, 6, 100) → sums — 100 two-dice sums at once.
  • Count: countIf(sums, 7) and countIf(sums, 12).
  • Compare with theory: Pr(7) = 636 ≈ 0.17, Pr(12) = 136 ≈ 0.03.

What you should see

Roughly six times as many 7s as 12s — the diagonal count made visible. If a student's simulation gives 7 and 12 about equally often, something's wrong with the code, not the dice.

08 · Summary 9C

Multi-stage experiments — what to remember

The tools

  • Tree diagram — any number of stages; read along branches, keep stage order.
  • Table — perfect for exactly two stages (e.g. the 36-cell dice grid).
  • Branch counts multiply: 2 × 6 = 12, 2 × 2 × 2 = 8.

The routine

List the whole sample space first.
Circle the outcomes in the event; probability = count ÷ total.
"At least one" → use the complement.

The #1 trap

Treating summaries of outcomes as equally likely. The 36 dice pairs are equally likely; the 11 sums are not. Always drop back to the level where outcomes really are equally likely.

Check · Mini-whiteboards

Check Hinge question — everyone shows an answer

On your whiteboard write A, B, C or D. On the count of three, hold it up — everyone answers at once.
A fair coin is tossed five times. Which sequence is most likely?
A  H T H T H
B  H H H H H
C  T T H H T
D  they are all equally likely
Answer: D. Each sequence is one path through the 32-outcome tree: probability 1/32 each. A and C "look more random", and B "looks impossible" — but looking random isn't being more likely. (Five heads as a total is rarer than three heads only because more sequences give three.)
