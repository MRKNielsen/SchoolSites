Roll two dice and add them. Which total is more likely?

On your whiteboard write A, B or C — go with your gut, then we'll test it.
You roll two ordinary dice and add the numbers. Is a total of…
A  7 more likely
B  11 more likely
C  equally likely
Answer: A — a total of 7 is far more likely. There are 6 ways to make 7 (1+6, 2+5, 3+4, 4+3, 5+2, 6+1) but only 2 ways to make 11 (5+6, 6+5) — out of 36 equally likely pairs. Choosing C is the classic equiprobability bias: the totals 2–12 are not equally likely, even though the 36 dice-pairs are.

That's today's big idea

Probability only equals n(A)/n(ε) when the sample space is built from equally likely outcomes — here that's the 36 pairs, not the 11 totals.

01 · The language of probability

Sample space ε, and events

Definitions

The sample space ε is the set of all possible outcomes of a random experiment. An event is a subset of the sample space — the outcomes we care about. We write both with set brackets { }.

Experiment → sample space

Roll a dieε = {1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6}
Toss two coinsε = {HH, HT, TH, TT}
Calls to your phone in 2 hoursε = {0, 1, 2, 3, …}

Events are subsets

  • ‘An even number’ on a die → {2, 4, 6}.
  • ‘Two heads’ with two coins → {HH} — a single outcome is still a set.
  • Capital letters A, B, C name events; n(A) counts the elements in A.
02 · Worked example

I Do Example 1

A bag contains seven marbles numbered 1 to 7, and one marble is withdrawn at random.

  1. a Give the sample space for this experiment.
  2. b List the outcomes of the event A = ‘a marble with an odd number is withdrawn’.
  3. c Write down n(A) and n(ε).
  • aEvery number from 1 to 7 could be drawn: ε = {1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7}.
  • bPick out the odd numbers: A = {1, 3, 5, 7}.
  • cn(A) = 4 and n(ε) = 7 — next we turn counts like these into a probability.

Set brackets are not optional

‘List the sample space’ means write the set: brackets, commas, every outcome once.

03 · Equally likely outcomes

I Do Example 2

Equally likely outcomes

If all n(ε) outcomes are equally likely, then for any event A:

Pr(A) = number of outcomes in Atotal number of outcomes = n(A)n(ε)

One letter is drawn at random from the letters of AUSTRALIA. Find the probability that the letter is:

  1. a a T
  2. b an A
  3. c a vowel
  • 1Count the outcomes. AUSTRALIA has 9 letters, so n(ε) = 9 — each of the 9 positions is equally likely, even though letters repeat.
  • 2a One T among 9 letters: Pr(T) = 19.
  • 3b Three A's: Pr(A) = 39 = 13.
  • 4c Vowels: A, U, A, I, A — five of them: Pr(vowel) = 59.

Repeats count separately

The event ‘an A’ contains three outcomes, not one. Count positions, not different letters.

04 · Complementary events

I Do Example 3

Complement

The complement A′ is everything in ε that is not in A. Together they cover the whole sample space, so

Pr(A′) = 1 − Pr(A)

A card is drawn at random from a pack of 52. Find the probability that the card is:

  1. a not a heart
  2. b not an ace

When to reach for A′

The word "not" — and later, "at least one" — are complement signals. Counting what you don't want is often faster.

  • 1a Let H = ‘a heart’. There are 13 hearts: Pr(H) = 1352 = 14.
  • 2So Pr(H′) = 1 − 14 = 34.
  • 3b Let A = ‘an ace’. Four aces: Pr(A) = 452 = 113.
  • 4So Pr(A′) = 1 − 113 = 1213.
05 · The probabilities sum to 1

We Do Example 4

Rules of probability

Pr(A) ≥ 0 for every event, and the probabilities of all outcomes add to 1. That second rule finds missing probabilities.

a A random experiment results in 1, 2, 3 or 4, with Pr(1) = 0.25, Pr(3) = 0.15 and Pr(4) = 0.3. Find Pr(2).

  • 1The four probabilities add to 1: 0.25 + Pr(2) + 0.15 + 0.3 = 1.
  • 2Pr(2) = 1 − 0.7 = 0.3.

b A different experiment results in A, B, C or D. Outcomes A, B, C are equally likely, and D is twice as likely as A. Find Pr(D) and Pr(B′).

  • 1Introduce one unknown. Let Pr(A) = Pr(B) = Pr(C) = x; then Pr(D) = 2x.
  • 2Sum to 1: x + x + x + 2x = 1, so 5x = 1 and x = 15.
  • 3Pr(D) = 2x = 25.
  • 4Pr(B′) = 1 − 15 = 45.

09 · Summary 9A

Sample spaces and probability — what to remember

The objects

  • Sample space ε: all possible outcomes, in set brackets.
  • Event: a subset of ε; n(A) counts its outcomes.
  • Equally likely: Pr(A) = n(A)n(ε).

The rules

Pr(A) ≥ 0, and all outcome probabilities sum to 1.
Complement: Pr(A′) = 1 − Pr(A) — the "not" shortcut.
Unknown probabilities: name one x, write the others in terms of it, sum to 1.

The #1 trap

Assuming outcomes are equally likely when they aren't. "Win or lose — so it's 50–50" is wrong unless the outcomes really are equally likely. Check how many ways each result can happen first.

Check · Mini-whiteboards

Check Hinge question — everyone shows an answer

On your whiteboard write A, B, C or D. On the count of three, hold it up — everyone answers at once.
A spinner lands on 1, 2 or 3. Pr(1) = 0.5 and Pr(2) = 0.3. What is Pr(3)?
A  0.2
B  13
C  0.7
D  0.5
Answer: A — 0.2. The three probabilities must sum to 1, so Pr(3) = 1 − 0.5 − 0.3. B assumed the three numbers are equally likely (they're not); C subtracted only Pr(2); D subtracted only Pr(1).
