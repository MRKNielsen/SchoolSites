01 · "Given" shrinks the world

I Do Example 1

500 people were asked whether they regularly use social media. One person is selected at random. Given that the person is under 25, find the probability that they regularly use social media.
  • 1Restrict: "given under 25" → ignore everyone else. Only the 240 under-25s remain in play.
  • 2Count inside: of those 240, 200 use social media.
  • 3Pr(Yes | under 25) = 200240 = 56 — the denominator is the condition's total, not 500.
Conditional probability
Pr(A | B) = Pr(A ∩ B)Pr(B) (Pr(B) ≠ 0)

Check: (200/500) ÷ (240/500) = 200/240. Same answer — the formula is the restriction.

02 · Direction matters

I Do Example 2

In a class, 12 students study Chinese, 20 study French, and 8 study both. a Given that a student studies Chinese, find the probability they also study French. b Given that a student studies French, find the probability they also study Chinese.
  • 1Venn regions: Chinese only = 12 − 8 = 4; both = 8; French only = 20 − 8 = 12.
  • aRestrict to the 12 Chinese students: Pr(F | C) = 812 = 23.
  • bRestrict to the 20 French students: Pr(C | F) = 820 = 25.
  • ✓Same overlap, different denominators: in general Pr(A | B) ≠ Pr(B | A).

Read it aloud

"The probability of F, given C" — whatever follows "given" is the new (smaller) world, and its count goes on the bottom.

03 · Using the formula

We Do Example 3

a For events A and B: Pr(A) = 0.7, Pr(B) = 0.3 and Pr(B | A) = 0.4. Find Pr(A ∩ B), then Pr(A | B).

  • 1Multiplication rule: Pr(A ∩ B) = Pr(B | A) × Pr(A) = 0.4 × 0.7 = 0.28.
  • 2Pr(A | B) = Pr(A ∩ B)Pr(B) = 0.280.3 = 1415.

b For events A and B: Pr(A) = 0.5, Pr(B) = 0.44 and Pr(A ∩ B′) = 0.3. Find Pr(A ∩ B), then Pr(A | B) and Pr(B | A).

  • 1Row subtraction (9E!): Pr(A ∩ B) = 0.5 − 0.3 = 0.2.
  • 2Pr(A | B) = 0.20.44 = 511.
  • 3Pr(B | A) = 0.20.5 = 0.4 — different again!
04 · Probability trees

I Do Example 4

In a certain town, Pr(rain Monday) = 0.21. If it rains Monday, Pr(rain Tuesday) = 0.83; if not, Pr(rain Tuesday) = 0.3. Find the probability that it rains: a on both days b on Tuesday.
Law of total probability
Pr(A) = Pr(A | B) Pr(B) + Pr(A | B′) Pr(B′)
  • 1Multiply along the branch: Pr(T ∩ M) = 0.21 × 0.83 = 0.1743. That's part a.
  • 2Tuesday rain also happens via "no rain Monday": Pr(T ∩ M′) = 0.79 × 0.3 = 0.237.
  • 3Add the branches that end in rain: Pr(T) = 0.1743 + 0.237 = 0.4113.

Tree grammar

Multiply along a branch (and-s), add across branches (or-s). Second-stage probabilities are conditionals.

06 · Your turn

You Do Example 5

a. Two dice are rolled. Given that the two numbers are the same, find the probability of a double six.
Restricted world = 6 doubles; one is (6,6): Pr = 16.
b. Pr(W) = 0.652 and Pr(A | W) = 0.354. Find Pr(A ∩ W) (a woman aged 25 or older is chosen).
Multiplication rule: 0.354 × 0.652 ≈ 0.231.
c. A team plays at home 60% of the time, winning 80% of home games and 40% of away games. Find Pr(win).
Total probability: 0.6 × 0.8 + 0.4 × 0.4 = 0.48 + 0.16 = 0.64.
d. What does Pr(A | B) = 1 tell you about A and B? What about Pr(A | B) = 0?
= 1: every outcome in B is in A, i.e. B ⊆ A. = 0: they share no outcomes — A and B are mutually exclusive.
07 · Summary 9F

Conditional probability — what to remember

The three tools

  • Restriction: count inside the "given" set only.
  • Formula: Pr(A | B) = Pr(A ∩ B)Pr(B); rearranged, Pr(A ∩ B) = Pr(A | B) × Pr(B).
  • Tree: multiply along, add across; Pr(A) = Pr(A|B)Pr(B) + Pr(A|B′)Pr(B′).

The routine

Underline what follows "given" — that's the denominator world.
Sequential story? Draw the tree. Survey data? Use the table or Venn.
Decimals feel slippery? Re-run the problem with 1000 people.

The #1 trap

Pr(A | B) is not Pr(B | A), and neither is Pr(A ∩ B). "Of the under-25s, how many use social media" (200/240), "of the users, how many are under 25" (200/300), and "under-25 users in the whole survey" (200/500) are three different numbers.
