Predict first · Vote — spot the error

Can you always just add two probabilities?

On your whiteboard write A, B or C — instinct first, then we'll check.
In a class, Pr(plays sport) = 0.6 and Pr(plays music) = 0.5. A student writes Pr(sport or music) = 0.6 + 0.5 = 1.1. What's gone wrong?
A  Nothing — for "or" you add.
B  You can only add when they can't both happen; here you must subtract the overlap.
C  You should have multiplied.
Answer: B. A probability can never exceed 1, so 1.1 is impossible. Some students play both, and they've been counted twice: Pr(sport or music) = 0.6 + 0.5 − Pr(both). You may add directly only when the events are mutually exclusive (no overlap). Treating every "or" as a plain sum is the misconception today fixes.

That's today's big idea

The addition rule Pr(A∪B) = Pr(A) + Pr(B) − Pr(A∩B) subtracts the double-counted overlap — and "mutually exclusive" is exactly the case where that overlap is zero.

Read the symbols aloud

∪ "or" — either or both. ∩ "and" — both at once. ′ "not". A ∩ B′ reads "A and not B" — the left crescent only.

02 · Venn diagrams from words

I Do Example 1

Fifty students were surveyed. 35 went to football matches or the movies (or both). Of the 22 who went to football, 12 also went to the movies. Find the probability that a randomly chosen student: a went to the movies but not football b went to neither.
  • 1Intersection first: n(F ∩ M) = 12 goes in the overlap.
  • 2Subtract outwards: football only = 22 − 12 = 10.
  • 3Union is 35, so movies only = 35 − 10 − 12 = 13; outside = 50 − 35 = 15.
  • aPr(F′ ∩ M) = 1350.
  • bPr(F′ ∩ M′) = 1550 = 310.

Never write 22 in one circle

22 is the whole football circle. Filling regions from the intersection outwards prevents double-counting.

03 · The addition rule

Why subtract the intersection? Because it was counted twice

counted in n(A)counted in n(B)counted twice
Addition rule
Pr(A ∪ B) = Pr(A) + Pr(B) − Pr(A ∩ B)

Always true — equally likely or not.

Mutually exclusive events

If A and B can't happen together (A ∩ B = ∅), then Pr(A ∩ B) = 0 and the rule collapses to Pr(A ∪ B) = Pr(A) + Pr(B).

04 · Applying the rule

I Do Example 2

One card is drawn at random from a pack of 52. What is the probability the card is a king or a spade?

  • 1Name the events. K = king: Pr(K) = 452. S = spade: Pr(S) = 1352.
  • 2Overlap? The king of spades is both: Pr(K ∩ S) = 152. Not mutually exclusive!
  • 3Pr(K ∪ S) = 452 + 1352 − 152 = 1652 = 413.

Check for the overlap first

"King or spade" tempts you to write 4 + 13 = 17 favourable cards — but that counts the king of spades twice. The addition rule automates the fix.

Contrast

"King or queen": a card can't be both, so Pr = 452 + 452 = 213 — mutually exclusive events just add.

05 · Rearranging the rule

We Do Example 3

a Given Pr(A) = 0.6, Pr(B) = 0.35 and Pr(A ∪ B) = 0.8, find Pr(A ∩ B).

  • 1Rearrange: Pr(A ∩ B) = Pr(A) + Pr(B) − Pr(A ∪ B).
  • 2= 0.6 + 0.35 − 0.8 = 0.15.

b Given Pr(A) = 0.3, Pr(B) = 0.52 and A ⊆ B (A is contained in B), find Pr(A ∩ B) and Pr(A ∪ B).

  • 1Picture it: the A circle sits wholly inside B.
  • 2Overlap is all of A: Pr(A ∩ B) = Pr(A) = 0.3.
  • 3Together they cover just B: Pr(A ∪ B) = Pr(B) = 0.52.

Draw before you calculate

Subset and mutually-exclusive setups are one-line answers if you sketch the picture first.

07 · Summary 9D

Combining events — what to remember

The language

  • A ∪ B: "or" — either or both.
  • A ∩ B: "and" — both at once.
  • A′: "not". Mutually exclusive: A ∩ B = ∅.

The routine

Draw the Venn; fill the intersection first, subtract outwards.
Addition rule: Pr(A ∪ B) = Pr(A) + Pr(B) − Pr(A ∩ B) — rearrange for any missing piece.
Subset or mutually exclusive? Sketch it — the answer is usually immediate.

The #1 trap

Adding Pr(A) + Pr(B) for "or" without subtracting the overlap. Only mutually exclusive events just add.

Check Hinge question — everyone shows an answer

On your whiteboard write A, B, C or D. On the count of three, hold it up — everyone answers at once.
Pr(A) = 0.5, Pr(B) = 0.4 and Pr(A ∪ B) = 0.7. What is Pr(A ∩ B)?
A  0.2
B  0.9
C  0
D  0.7
Answer: A — 0.2. Rearrange the addition rule: 0.5 + 0.4 − 0.7. B added the two probabilities (that's Pr(A) + Pr(B), not the intersection); C assumed mutually exclusive without evidence; D confused ∩ with ∪.
