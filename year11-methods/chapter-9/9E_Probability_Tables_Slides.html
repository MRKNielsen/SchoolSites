Predict first · Vote — trust the counts

“Owns a cat” — out of everyone, or out of the dog owners?

On your whiteboard write A, B or C — predict, then we'll count it out.
In a town of 1000 people, 100 own a dog. Of those dog owners, 40 also own a cat. You pick a dog owner at random. What's the chance they own a cat?
A  4%
B  40%
C  44%
Answer: B — 40%. We're told the person is a dog owner, so we only look inside that group of 100: 40 out of 100 = 40%. Answer A (40/1000 = 4%) is the chance of owning both out of everyone — the classic mix-up of a joint "and" with a conditional "given". Whole-number counts make the difference obvious.

That's today's big idea

A probability table is just these counts laid out in a grid. "Out of everyone" reads from the total; "given that…" reads from a single row or column.

01 · Counts before probabilities

A 100-student table — the four regions become four cells

From Venn to table

The four Venn regions A ∩ B, A ∩ B′, A′ ∩ B, A′ ∩ B′ become the four cells. Rows add to Pr(A) and Pr(A′); columns add to Pr(B) and Pr(B′); the corner is 1.

Why counts first?

"53 of the 100 students play sport and were born here" is easier to reason about than "0.53". Whole-number tables make the structure obvious — then divide by the total to get probabilities. Use this move whenever decimals confuse.

02 · Completing a table

I Do Example 1

Events A and B have Pr(A) = 0.7, Pr(A ∩ B) = 0.4 and Pr(A′ ∩ B) = 0.2. Find: a Pr(A ∩ B′) b Pr(B) c Pr(A′ ∩ B′) d Pr(A ∪ B)
  • 1Place the givens: 0.4 in the A ∩ B cell, 0.2 below it, 0.7 as the A row total.
  • aAcross row 1: Pr(A ∩ B′) = 0.7 − 0.4 = 0.3.
  • bDown column 1: Pr(B) = 0.4 + 0.2 = 0.6.
  • cPr(A′) = 0.3, so Pr(A′ ∩ B′) = 0.3 − 0.2 = 0.1.
  • dAddition rule: Pr(A ∪ B) = 0.7 + 0.6 − 0.4 = 0.9 — or 1 − 0.1 from the table. Same answer.
03 · From words to table

We Do Example 2

In a survey: 65% of students play sport (S), 71% were born in Australia (A), and 53% are Australian-born and play sport. Find the probability that a random student:

  1. a was not born in Australia
  2. b was not born in Australia and does not play sport.
  • 1Fill the givens: cell Pr(A ∩ S) = 0.53; margins Pr(A) = 0.71, Pr(S) = 0.65.
  • 2Subtract everywhere: row 1 gives 0.18; column 1 gives 0.12; margins give Pr(A′) = 0.29, Pr(S′) = 0.35.
  • aPr(A′) = 0.29 — straight off the margin.
  • bLast cell: Pr(A′ ∩ S′) = 0.29 − 0.12 = 0.17. (Check: all four cells sum to 1. ✓)

"And" goes in a cell, totals go in margins

The 53% is an intersection — a cell. The 65% and 71% are single-event totals — margins. Placing givens correctly is most of the work.

05 · Your turn

You Do Example 3

a. Pr(A) = 0.6, Pr(A ∩ B) = 0.4, Pr(A′ ∩ B) = 0.1. Find Pr(A ∩ B′) and Pr(B).
Row: 0.6 − 0.4 = 0.2. Column: 0.4 + 0.1 = 0.5.
b. Continuing part a: find Pr(A′ ∩ B′) and Pr(A ∪ B).
Pr(A′) = 0.4, so 0.4 − 0.1 = 0.3; Pr(A ∪ B) = 1 − 0.3 = 0.7.
c. 85% of seniors favour proposition P, 60% favour Q, 50% favour both. What percentage favour at least one?
0.85 + 0.6 − 0.5 = 0.95 — so 95%.
d. And what percentage favour neither?
1 − 0.95 = 0.05 — 5%. "Neither" is the complement of "at least one".
06 · Summary 9E

Probability tables — what to remember

The layout

  • Four cells = the four "and" combinations of A/A′ with B/B′.
  • Rows and columns add to the margins; the corner is 1.
  • Stuck on decimals? Rebuild the table with counts out of 100.

The routine

Place the givens: "and" → a cell; a single event → a margin.
Subtract along rows and columns until full.
Check: the four cells sum to 1. Then read off the answer.

The #1 trap

Putting Pr(A) in a cell. A margin total is not an "and" probability — the cell holds Pr(A ∩ B), the margin holds Pr(A).

Check · Mini-whiteboards

Check Hinge question — everyone shows an answer

On your whiteboard write A, B, C or D. On the count of three, hold it up — everyone answers at once.
From the table, Pr(A) = ?
A  0.5
B  0.3
C  0.7
D  0.6
Answer: A — 0.5. Pr(A) = 0.3 + 0.2, the whole first row. B read a single cell (that's Pr(A ∩ B)); C added the wrong pair (first column, Pr(B)); D added a row and a cell.
